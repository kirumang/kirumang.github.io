🌐 Research Landscape

Three of today's twenty papers center on the inner workings of Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models, marking a clear maturation of the field: the question has shifted from "do VLAs work?" to "why do they work, and how do we make them faster and more honest to measure?" The trio forms a coherent arc. Paper #8 (Embodied Efficiency) demolishes standard efficiency metrics — FLOPs, parameters, token throughput — showing they are divorced from real deployment costs like task completion time, trajectory jerk, and energy. Paper #17 (FASTER) then targets the most acute bottleneck the field ignores: reaction latency in flow-based policies, compressing near-term action denoising from ~10 steps to one via a Horizon-Aware Schedule. And paper #20 (Not All Features Are Equal) dissects VLA internals mechanistically across six models and 394,000+ rollouts, finding that visual pathways overwhelmingly drive action generation, language is invoked only when scene context is ambiguous, and distinct motor vs. goal subspaces exist in multi-pathway architectures. Together, these papers argue that the field needs richer evaluation frameworks, lower-latency inference paths, and greater interpretability before VLAs can be reliably trusted in the real world.

A second major thread is the growing case for tactile sensing as a first-class modality. Papers #2 (ViTac-Tracing) and #19 (OmniVTA) independently arrive at the same conclusion: vision alone cannot reliably ground dexterous manipulation in contact dynamics. ViTac-Tracing solves 1D and 2D deformable object tracing with a unified visual-tactile model, while OmniVTA goes further with a 21,000+ trajectory dataset organized by physics-grounded interaction patterns and a world model that predicts contact evolution at 60 Hz for closed-loop control. Both papers underscore that the field's vision-centric data collection and evaluation paradigm systematically undersells what embodied manipulation actually requires — and together they provide both the architectural solutions and the data infrastructure to address it.

A third theme is bridging the simulation-to-reality gap from multiple angles simultaneously. Paper #5 (ABD-Net) embeds articulated-body dynamics as a structural inductive bias in RL policy networks, improving sample efficiency and sim-to-real transfer on humanoid and quadruped platforms. Paper #11 (V-Dreamer) automates simulation environment and trajectory generation by treating video generation models as motion priors, removing the need for manual scene setup. Paper #16 (Fire as a Service) fills a specific vacuum: thermally and visually accurate fire co-simulation for firefighting robot training. And paper #6 (Quadrupedal Skating) uses bilevel Bayesian Optimization + RL to jointly discover hardware designs and locomotion controllers, producing emergent behaviors (hockey stops, self-alignment) that neither designer alone would have found. Collectively, these papers suggest the community is converging on a view that rich, automated simulation infrastructure is a prerequisite for scalable robot learning, not an afterthought.

Fundamental Limits for Sensor-Based Control via the Gibbs Variational Principle
2026-03-19 math.OC cs.RO eess.SY Evangelos A. Theodorou (h=47)
Vincent Pacelli · Evangelos A. Theodorou
Core Contributions
  • Existing information-theoretic control bounds evaluate sensor quality against the uncontrolled system — essentially grading a sensor assuming the robot does nothing — which makes bounds overly loose precisely when feedback control matters most. The Gibbs approach conditions on the controlled path measure, correcting this fundamental error.
  • Derives a tight lower bound on minimum achievable cost for any causal feedback controller under partial observations, applicable to nonlinear, nonholonomic, and hybrid dynamics with unbounded costs — far broader scope than prior linear-system results.
  • A self-consistent refinement loop is key: a better controller concentrates the state distribution, which limits what the sensor can observe, which tightens the bound — yielding a unique fixed point computable by bisection rather than costly global optimization.
  • Provides convexity conditions under which free-energy minimization is certifiably correct numerically, giving practitioners not just a bound but a provably tight certificate before deploying a sensor-controller pair.
  • On a nonlinear Dubins car tracking problem, the self-consistent bound captures most of the optimal cost across all sensor noise levels, while the open-loop variant yields vacuous (useless) bounds at low noise — demonstrating the practical gap the new method closes.
Fundamental limits on the performance of feedback controllers are essential for benchmarking algorithms, guiding sensor selection, and certifying task feasibility -- yet few general-purpose tools exist for computing them. Existing information-theoretic approaches overestimate the information a sensor must provide by evaluating it against the uncontrolled system, producing bounds that degrade precisely when feedback is most valuable. We derive a lower bound on the minimum expected cost of any causal feedback controller under partial observations by applying the Gibbs variational principle to the joint path measure over states and observations. The bound applies to nonlinear, nonholonomic, and hybrid dynamics with unbounded costs and admits a self-consistent refinement: any good controller concentrates the state, which limits the information the sensor can extract, which tightens the bound. The resulting fixed-point equation has a unique solution computable by bisection, and we provide conditions under which the free energy minimization is provably convex, yielding a certifiably correct numerical bound. On a nonlinear Dubins car tracking problem, the self-consistent bound captures most of the optimal cost across sensor noise levels, while the open-loop variant is vacuous at low noise.

Tendon-Actuated Robots with a Tapered, Flexible Polymer Backbone: Design, Fabrication, and Modeling
2026-03-19 cs.RO J. Gravdahl (h=45)
Harald Minde Hansen · Nandita Gallacher · Nicholas B. Andrews · Kristin Y. Pettersen · Jan Tommy Gravdahl
Core Contributions
  • Most continuum robots are expensive, single-purpose, and hard to replicate — this design uses 3D-printed TPU backbones with fully parameterized CAD scripts, enabling rapid geometry generation and scaling on any standard FDM printer without specialized equipment or materials.
  • Geometric tapering provides graded stiffness (stiffer at the base for load-bearing, more compliant at the tip for dexterous tasks) — a configuration space benefit that uniform-cross-section backbones cannot achieve without adding mass or extra actuators.
  • Extends the standard Cosserat rod kinetostatic model to explicitly account for spatially varying cross-sectional geometry, enabling systematic analysis of how taper angle controls configuration space and manipulability — a gap in existing tendon-actuated formulations.
  • Shape prediction achieves centimeter-level accuracy against motion-capture ground truth after calibrating Young's modulus via a simple line-search minimization — pragmatic enough to replicate without dedicated material testing rigs.

This paper presents the design, modeling, and fabrication of 3D-printed, tendon-actuated continuum robots featuring a flexible, tapered backbone constructed from thermoplastic polyurethane (TPU). Our scalable design incorporates an integrated electronics base housing that enables direct tendon tension control and sensing via actuators and compression load cells. Unlike many continuum robots that are single-purpose and costly, the proposed design prioritizes customizability, rapid assembly, and low cost while enabling high curvature and enhanced distal compliance through geometric tapering, thereby supporting a broad range of compliant robotic inspection and manipulation tasks. We develop a generalized forward kinetostatic model of the tapered backbone based on Cosserat rod theory using a Newtonian approach, extending existing tendon-actuated Cosserat rod formulations to explicitly account for spatially varying backbone cross-sectional geometry. The model captures the graded stiffness profile induced by the tapering and enables systematic exploration of the configuration space as a function of the geometric design parameters. The model is validated against motion capture data, achieving centimeter-level shape prediction accuracy after calibrating Young's modulus via a line search that minimizes modeling error. We further demonstrate teleoperated grasping using an endoscopic gripper routed along the continuum robot, mounted on a 6-DoF robotic arm. Parameterized iLogic/CAD scripts are provided for rapid geometry generation and scaling.

ViTac-Tracing: Visual-Tactile Imitation Learning of Deformable Object Tracing
2026-03-19 cs.RO Y. Demiris (h=46)
Yongqiang Zhao · Haining Luo · Yupeng Wang · Emmanouil Spyrakos Papastavridis · Yiannis Demiris
Core Contributions
  • Tracing deformable objects (ropes, cables, cloth edges) requires local contact feedback that cameras cannot reliably provide — this is the first unified visual-tactile model handling both 1D (cable following) and 2D (surface tracing) tasks without category-specific engineering or per-object models.
  • A weighted imitation loss that emphasizes actions maintaining contact near the center of the tactile image directly encodes the fundamental constraint for tracing: stay in contact — preventing the common failure of drifting off the object without requiring explicit programming of contact constraints.
  • A global "tracing task loss" regulates task progression across the whole trajectory, complementing local tactile feedback and preventing the policy from getting stuck in contact-maintaining loops that make no forward progress.
  • Tactile sensing is integrated into the teleoperation system itself (not just the robot), ensuring demonstrators feel what the robot feels — critically, training demonstrations thereby exhibit the tactile-informed behaviors the policy is expected to learn.
  • Achieves 80% average success on seen objects and 65% on unseen objects — competitive with specialist per-object methods while using a single generalizable policy, demonstrating meaningful cross-category transfer.
Deformable objects often appear in unstructured configurations. Tracing deformable objects helps bringing them into extended states and facilitating the downstream manipulation tasks. Due to the requirements for object-specific modeling or sim-to-real transfer, existing tracing methods either lack generalizability across different categories of deformable objects or struggle to complete tasks reliably in the real world. To address this, we propose a novel visual-tactile imitation learning method to achieve one-dimensional (1D) and two-dimensional (2D) deformable object tracing with a unified model. Our method is designed from both local and global perspectives based on visual and tactile sensing. Locally, we introduce a weighted loss that emphasizes actions maintaining contact near the center of the tactile image, improving fine-grained adjustment. Globally, we propose a tracing task loss that helps the policy to regulate task progression. On the hardware side, to compensate for the limited features extracted from visual information, we integrate tactile sensing into a low-cost teleoperation system considering both the teleoperator and the robot. Extensive ablation and comparative experiments on diverse 1D and 2D deformable objects demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach, achieving an average success rate of 80% on seen objects and 65% on unseen objects.

OmniVTA: Visuo-Tactile World Modeling for Contact-Rich Robotic Manipulation
2026-03-19 cs.RO Yuhang Zheng (h=8)
Yuhang Zheng · Songen Gu · Weize Li · Yupeng Zheng · Yujie Zang
Core Contributions
  • OmniViTac fills a critical data gap: 21,000+ trajectories across 86 tasks and 100+ objects organized into six physics-grounded interaction patterns (wiping, assembly, etc.) — dwarfing prior visuo-tactile datasets and providing the scale needed for systematic training.
  • The key paradigm shift: treating tactile signals as active feedback for a 60 Hz reflexive controller rather than passive state observations appended to visual features — the robot corrects deviations between predicted and observed contact in real time, not after the fact.
  • A two-stream world model predicts short-horizon contact evolution from current state, letting the robot anticipate slipping or loss of contact before it happens — enabling proactive rather than purely reactive contact management.
  • Physics-grounded organization of training data by interaction type (rather than by object or task) measurably improves generalization to unseen objects and geometric configurations — suggesting task taxonomy is a first-class design variable in data collection.
  • The tightly coupled four-module pipeline (tactile encoder → world model → fusion policy → reflexive controller) outperforms existing methods across all six interaction categories, confirming that predictive contact modeling is the key differentiator over standard vision-action baselines.
Contact-rich manipulation tasks, such as wiping and assembly, require accurate perception of contact forces, friction changes, and state transitions that cannot be reliably inferred from vision alone. Despite growing interest in visuo-tactile manipulation, progress is constrained by two persistent limitations: existing datasets are small in scale and narrow in task coverage, and current methods treat tactile signals as passive observations rather than using them to model contact dynamics or enable closed-loop control explicitly. In this paper, we present OmniViTac, a large-scale visuo-tactile-action dataset comprising 21,000+ trajectories across 86 tasks and 100+ objects, organized into six physics-grounded interaction patterns. Building on this dataset, we propose OmniVTA, a world-model-based visuo-tactile manipulation framework that integrates four tightly coupled modules: a self-supervised tactile encoder, a two-stream visuo-tactile world model for predicting short-horizon contact evolution, a contact-aware fusion policy for action generation, and a 60Hz reflexive controller that corrects deviations between predicted and observed tactile signals in a closed loop. Real-robot experiments across all six interaction categories show that OmniVTA outperforms existing methods and generalizes well to unseen objects and geometric configurations.

We introduce MERGE, a system for situational grounding of actors, objects, and events in dynamic human-robot group interactions. Central to MERGE is the integration of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) guided with a perception pipeline: a lightweight streaming module continuously processes visual input to detect changes and selectively invokes the VLM only when necessary. Articulated-Body Dynamics Network: Dynamics-Grounded Prior for Robot Learning
2026-03-19 cs.RO Josiah P. Hanna (h=22)
Sangwoo Shin · Kunzhao Ren · Xiaobin Xiong · Josiah Hanna
Core Contributions
  • Prior structural priors for RL (link connectivity graphs) capture what is connected but ignore how forces propagate — ABD-Net adapts the Articulated Body Algorithm's child-to-parent inertia propagation as a learnable network layer, embedding dynamics rather than just kinematics as an inductive bias.
  • Physical quantities in the ABA are replaced with learnable parameters, so the network benefits from the dynamics computational structure without requiring accurate system identification or known physical parameters — making it practical for real hardware.
  • The dynamics-grounded representation captures how actions propagate through the body, producing more physically meaningful intermediate features than generic transformer or GNN architectures trained from scratch on the same reinforcement learning data.
  • Demonstrates improved sample efficiency and better generalization to dynamics shifts (perturbations to inertia, friction, etc.) vs. transformer and GNN baselines across three morphologically distinct systems: humanoid, quadruped, and hopper.
  • Successfully validated with real-time sim-to-real transfer on both Unitree G1 (humanoid) and Go2 (quadruped) platforms, confirming the approach works across mechanically distinct systems without any architecture changes between morphologies.
Recent work in reinforcement learning has shown that incorporating structural priors for articulated robots, such as link connectivity, into policy networks improves learning efficiency. However, dynamics properties, despite their fundamental role in determining how forces and motion propagate through the body, remain largely underexplored as an inductive bias for policy learning. To address this gap, we present the Articulated-Body Dynamics Network (ABD-Net), a novel graph neural network architecture grounded in the computational structure of forward dynamics. Specifically, we adapt the inertia propagation mechanism from the Articulated Body Algorithm, systematically aggregating inertial quantities from child to parent links in a tree-structured manner, while replacing physical quantities with learnable parameters. Embedding ABD-NET into the policy actor enables dynamics-informed representations that capture how actions propagate through the body, leading to efficient and robust policy learning. Through experiments with simulated humanoid, quadruped, and hopper robots, our approach demonstrates increased sample efficiency and generalization to dynamics shifts compared to transformer-based and GNN baselines. We further validate the learned policy on real Unitree G1 and Go2 robots, generating dynamic, versatile and robust locomotion behaviors through sim-to-real transfer with real-time inference.

2026-03-19 cs.RO math.OC K. Hamed (h=19)
Yicheng Zeng · Ruturaj S. Sambhus · Basit Muhammad Imran · Jeeseop Kim · Vittorio Pastore
Core Contributions
  • CBF safety constraints create inter-agent coupling that prevents standard distributed decomposition — this paper solves it via a node-edge splitting ADMM formulation that separates per-robot QPs (node-local) from per-pair QPs (edge-local), enabling true parallelization with only neighbor-to-neighbor communication.

This paper proposes a fully decentralized model predictive control (MPC) framework with control barrier function (CBF) constraints for safety-critical trajectory planning in multi-robot legged systems.
