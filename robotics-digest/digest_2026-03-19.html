📊 Research Landscape

Today's top 20 robotics papers reflect three broad trends shaping the field in early 2026. Foundation models are now core infrastructure — VLAs, LLMs, and video generation models appear across manipulation, navigation, simulation, and multi-robot planning, with dedicated work beginning to analyze why and how these models work internally (mechanistic interpretability for robotics is emerging). Closing the sensing gap is another major theme: tactile sensing is being integrated at both the dataset and architecture levels to handle contact-rich tasks that vision alone cannot resolve. System-level co-design — jointly optimizing hardware, control, and learning — appears in quadruped skating, continuum robots, EV disassembly, and simulation frameworks, signaling a shift from algorithm-first thinking toward full-system engineering.

A recurring gap exposed by today's papers: conventional metrics (FLOPs, parameters, success rate) increasingly fail to capture what matters in real deployment. Multiple papers call for or introduce new evaluation frameworks covering embodied efficiency, metric-semantic grounding, and mechanistic behavior analysis.

From Inference Efficiency to Embodied Efficiency: Revisiting Efficiency Metrics for Vision-Language-Action Models
2026-03-19 cs.LG · cs.RO Top: Chaojian Li (h=19)
Zhuofan Li, Hongkun Yang, Zhenyang Chen, Yangxuan Chen, Yingyan et al.
Core Contributions
  • Demonstrates that FLOPs, parameter count, and token throughput — the dominant VLA efficiency metrics — do not predict actual on-robot performance or execution quality.
  • Introduces embodied efficiency metrics: task completion time, trajectory smoothness, cumulative joint rotation, and motion energy — measured through controlled real-robot studies.
  • Shows that inference-efficient methods (compression, token sparsification) often increase end-to-end execution cost or degrade motion quality while maintaining success rates.
  • Fine-tuning and in-context prompting improve targeted embodied metrics but introduce trade-offs in others (e.g., less jerk but longer completion time).
  • Argues for system-level evaluation as the standard for fair VLA comparison, not algorithm-level proxies.
Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models have recently enabled embodied agents to perform increasingly complex tasks by jointly reasoning over visual, linguistic, and motor modalities. However, we find that the prevailing notion of "efficiency" in current VLA research, characterized by parameters, FLOPs, or token decoding throughput, does not reflect actual performance on robotic platforms. In real-world execution, efficiency is determined by system-level embodied behaviors such as task completion time, trajectory smoothness, cumulative joint rotation, and motion energy. Through controlled studies across model compression, token sparsification, and action sequence compression, we make several observations that challenge common assumptions. (1) Methods that reduce computation under conventional metrics often increase end-to-end execution cost or degrade motion quality, despite maintaining task success rates. (2) System-level embodied efficiency metrics reveal performance differences in the learned action policies that remain hidden under conventional evaluations. (3) Common adaptation methods such as in-context prompting or supervised fine-tuning show only mild and metric-specific improvements in embodied efficiency.

FASTER: Rethinking Real-Time Flow VLAs
2026-03-19 cs.RO · cs.CV Top: Hengshuang Zhao (h=9)
Yuxiang Lu, Zhe Liu, Xianzhe Fan, Zhenya Yang, Jinghua Hou
Core Contributions
  • Identifies reaction time (not throughput) as the critical real-time bottleneck in VLA deployment, decomposed as a uniform distribution over Time to First Action (TTFA) and execution horizon.
  • Reveals that standard constant denoising schedules in flow-based VLAs force all sampling steps to complete before any motion starts — making this the dominant latency source.
  • Proposes a Horizon-Aware Schedule that adaptively front-loads near-term action denoising, compressing the first-action step to a single denoising pass (10× compression vs. baselines like π₀.₅ and X-VLA).
  • Pairs with a streaming client-server pipeline to minimize inference-to-actuation delay on consumer-grade GPUs.
  • Validated on a dynamic table tennis task requiring sub-second reaction — a task that flat-schedule VLAs cannot handle at all.
Real-time execution is crucial for deploying Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models in the physical world. Existing asynchronous inference methods primarily optimize trajectory smoothness, but neglect the critical latency in reacting to environmental changes. By rethinking the notion of reaction in action chunking policies, this paper presents a systematic analysis of the factors governing reaction time. We show that reaction time follows a uniform distribution determined jointly by the Time to First Action (TTFA) and the execution horizon. Moreover, we reveal that the standard practice of applying a constant schedule in flow-based VLAs can be inefficient and forces the system to complete all sampling steps before any movement can start, forming the bottleneck in reaction latency. To overcome this issue, we propose Fast Action Sampling for ImmediaTE Reaction (FASTER). By introducing a Horizon-Aware Schedule, FASTER adaptively prioritizes near-term actions during flow sampling, compressing the denoising of the immediate reaction by tenfold into a single step, while preserving the quality of long-horizon trajectory.

Not All Features Are Created Equal: A Mechanistic Study of Vision-Language-Action Models
2026-03-19 cs.RO Top: Xijia Zhao (h=7)
Bryce Grant, Xijia Zhao, Peng Wang
Core Contributions
  • First mechanistic interpretability study of VLA models at scale: 6 models (80M–7B params) analyzed via activation injection, sparse autoencoders, and linear probes across 394,000+ rollout episodes.
  • Visual pathway dominates action generation in all tested architectures — injecting baseline visual activations into null-prompt episodes recovers near-identical motor behavior.
  • Language sensitivity is task-structure-dependent, not model-dependent: language is ignored when vision uniquely determines the goal; becomes essential when multiple goals share the same visual scene.
  • Expert and VLM pathways encode separable representations: expert pathways carry motor programs (2× behavioral displacement); VLM pathways carry goal semantics — confirmed via subspace injection.
  • Releases Action Atlas (action-atlas.com) for interactive exploration of VLA representations across all six models.
Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models combine perception, language, and motor control in a single architecture, yet how they translate multimodal inputs into actions remains poorly understood. We apply activation injection, sparse autoencoders (SAEs), and linear probes to six models spanning 80M–7B parameters across 394,000+ rollout episodes on four benchmarks. The visual pathway dominates action generation across all architectures: injecting baseline activations into null-prompt episodes recovers near-identical behavior, while cross-task injection steers robots toward source-task positions, exposing spatially bound motor programs tied to scene coordinates rather than abstract task representations. Language sensitivity depends on task structure, not model design: when visual context uniquely specifies the task, language is ignored; when multiple goals share a scene, language becomes essential.

2026-03-19 cs.RO Top: Y. Demiris (h=46)
Yongqiang Zhao, Haining Luo, Yupeng Wang, Emmanouil Spyrakos Papastavridis, Yiannis Demiris
Core Contributions
  • Unified imitation learning policy for tracing both 1D (cables, ropes) and 2D (cloth, fabric) deformable objects without category-specific models or sim-to-real transfer.
  • Local weighted loss emphasizes actions that maintain tactile contact near the sensor center, improving fine-grained adjustment at the contact point.
  • Global tracing task loss regularizes long-horizon progression, preventing the policy from stalling or drifting off the deformable surface.
  • Tactile sensing integrated into a low-cost teleoperation system on both the operator and robot sides, enriching training data with contact signals unavailable from vision alone.

Deformable objects often appear in unstructured configurations. Tracing deformable objects helps bringing them into extended states and facilitating the downstream manipulation tasks. Due to the requirements for object-specific modeling or sim-to-real transfer, existing tracing methods either lack generalizability across different categories of deformable objects or struggle to complete tasks reliably in the real world. To address this, we propose a novel visual-tactile imitation learning method to achieve one-dimensional (1D) and two-dimensional (2D) deformable object tracing with a unified model. Our method is designed from both local and global perspectives based on visual and tactile sensing. Extensive ablation and comparative experiments on diverse 1D and 2D deformable objects demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach, achieving an average success rate of 80% on seen objects and 65% on unseen objects.

2026-03-19 cs.RO Top: Yuhang Zheng (h=8)
Yuhang Zheng, Songen Gu, Weize Li, Yupeng Zheng, Yujie Zang
Core Contributions
  • OmniViTac: largest visuo-tactile-action dataset to date — 21,000+ trajectories across 86 tasks, 100+ objects, organized into 6 physics-grounded interaction patterns (wiping, assembly, etc.).
  • Two-stream world model jointly predicts short-horizon contact evolution from vision and tactile streams, enabling the policy to reason about future contact states rather than react to them.
  • 60Hz reflexive controller closes the control loop by comparing predicted vs. observed tactile signals and correcting deviations in real time — much faster than typical policy inference rates.
  • Self-supervised tactile encoder trained without manual labels, reducing annotation cost for new sensor deployments.
  • Outperforms existing methods across all 6 interaction categories and generalizes to unseen objects and geometric configurations.
Contact-rich manipulation tasks, such as wiping and assembly, require accurate perception of contact forces, friction changes, and state transitions that cannot be reliably inferred from vision alone. Despite growing interest in visuo-tactile manipulation, progress is constrained by two persistent limitations: existing datasets are small in scale and narrow in task coverage, and current methods treat tactile signals as passive observations rather than using them to model contact dynamics or enable closed-loop control explicitly. In this paper, we present OmniViTac, a large-scale visuo-tactile-action dataset comprising 21,000+ trajectories across 86 tasks and 100+ objects, organized into six physics-grounded interaction patterns. Building on this dataset, we propose OmniVTA, a world-model-based visuo-tactile manipulation framework that integrates a self-supervised tactile encoder, a two-stream visuo-tactile world model, a contact-aware fusion policy, and a 60Hz reflexive controller that corrects deviations between predicted and observed tactile signals in a closed loop.

Articulated-Body Dynamics Network: Dynamics-Grounded Prior for Robot Learning
2026-03-19 cs.RO Top: Josiah P. Hanna (h=22)
Sangwoo Shin, Kunzhao Ren, Xiaobin Xiong, Josiah Hanna
Core Contributions
  • ABD-Net: a GNN architecture whose computational structure mirrors the Articulated Body Algorithm — inertia aggregated child-to-parent in a kinematic tree, but with learnable parameters replacing physical quantities.
  • Provides a dynamics inductive bias: the network naturally models how actuator forces propagate through the kinematic chain, without needing explicit physical simulation during inference.
  • Outperforms transformer and standard GNN baselines in sample efficiency on humanoid, quadruped, and hopper locomotion tasks.
  • Better generalization to dynamics shifts (e.g., payload changes, ground friction) compared to architecture-agnostic baselines.
  • Successfully deployed sim-to-real on Unitree G1 (humanoid) and Go2 (quadruped) with real-time inference.
Recent work in reinforcement learning has shown that incorporating structural priors for articulated robots, such as link connectivity, into policy networks improves learning efficiency. However, dynamics properties, despite their fundamental role in determining how forces and motion propagate through the body, remain largely underexplored as an inductive bias for policy learning. To address this gap, we present the Articulated-Body Dynamics Network (ABD-Net), a novel graph neural network architecture grounded in the computational structure of forward dynamics. Specifically, we adapt the inertia propagation mechanism from the Articulated Body Algorithm, systematically aggregating inertial quantities from child to parent links in a tree-structured manner, while replacing physical quantities with learnable parameters. Embedding ABD-NET into the policy actor enables dynamics-informed representations that capture how actions propagate through the body, leading to efficient and robust policy learning.

Efficient and Versatile Quadrupedal Skating: Optimal Co-design via Reinforcement Learning and Bayesian Optimization
2026-03-19 cs.RO Top: Josiah P. Hanna (h=22)
Hanwen Wang, Zhenlong Fang, Josiah Hanna, Xiaobin Xiong
Core Contributions
  • First system-level study of dynamic roller skating on quadruped robots, using passive (non-actuated) wheels to reduce leg inertia and improve energy efficiency at high speeds.
  • Bilevel optimization: upper-level Bayesian Optimization searches the mechanical design space; lower-level RL trains a motor control policy for each candidate design — neither is fixed independently.
  • Co-designed system discovers emergent skating behaviors: hockey stop (sideways friction braking) and self-aligning motion (automatic reorientation for efficiency in travel direction).
  • Passive-wheel coupling means mechanical design and control are fundamentally interdependent — the paper formalizes and solves this joint problem.
  • Outperforms human-engineered design baselines in both efficiency metrics and behavior versatility.
In this paper, we present a hardware-control co-design approach that enables efficient and versatile roller skating on quadrupedal robots equipped with passive wheels. Passive-wheel skating reduces leg inertia and improves energy efficiency, particularly at high speeds. However, the absence of direct wheel actuation tightly couples mechanical design and control. To unlock the full potential of this modality, we formulate a bilevel optimization framework: an upper-level Bayesian Optimization searches the mechanical design space, while a lower-level Reinforcement Learning trains a motor control policy for each candidate design. The resulting design-policy pairs not only outperform human-engineered baselines, but also exhibit versatile behaviors such as hockey stop (rapid braking by turning sideways to maximize friction) and self-aligning motion.

REST: Receding Horizon Explorative Steiner Tree for Zero-Shot Object-Goal Navigation
2026-03-19 cs.RO · cs.AI · cs.CV Top: Maani Ghaffari (h=17)
Shuqi Xiao, Maani Ghaffari, Chengzhong Xu, Hui Kong
Core Contributions
  • Key insight: navigation subgoals should be trees of full paths, not isolated waypoints — full paths expose information gain along the route that destination-only scoring systematically misses.
  • Grows a Steiner tree of shared path segments as the option space, enabling coarse-to-fine LLM reasoning that prunes entire branches before evaluating individual destinations.
  • Textualizes each branch into a spatial narrative, making the path space legible to chain-of-thought LLM reasoning without task-specific training.
  • Builds an online open-vocabulary 3D map from RGB-D streams — fully training-free, no pre-built maps or fine-tuning needed.
  • Top-tier success rate with best/second-best path efficiency across Gibson, HM3D, and HSSD benchmarks.
Zero-shot object-goal navigation (ZSON) requires navigating unknown environments to find a target object without task-specific training. Prior hierarchical training-free solutions invest in scene understanding (belief) and high-level decision-making (policy), yet overlook the design of option, i.e., a subgoal candidate proposed from evolving belief and presented to policy for selection. In practice, options are reduced to isolated waypoints scored independently: single destinations hide the value gathered along the journey; an unstructured collection obscures the relationships among candidates. Our insight is that the option space should be a tree of paths. Full paths expose en-route information gain that destination-only scoring systematically neglects; a tree of shared segments enables coarse-to-fine LLM reasoning that dismisses or pursues entire branches before examining individual leaves.

Central to MERGE is the integration of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) guided with a perception pipeline: a lightweight streaming module continuously processes visual input to detect changes and selectively invokes the VLM only when necessary.   • Handles unknown dynamic objects and highly cluttered scenes that defeat prior dynamic SLAM methods relying on predefined priors (e.g., pedestrian or vehicle detectors).

We present a robust, real-time RGB SLAM system that handles dynamic environments by leveraging differentiable Uncertainty-aware Bundle Adjustment. Traditional SLAM methods typically assume static scenes, leading to tracking failures in the presence of motion. Recent dynamic SLAM approaches attempt to address this challenge using predefined dynamic priors or uncertainty-aware mapping, but they remain limited when confronted with unknown dynamic objects or highly cluttered scenes where geometric mapping becomes unreliable. In contrast, our method estimates per-pixel uncertainty by exploiting multi-view visual feature inconsistency, enabling robust tracking and reconstruction even in real-world environments. The proposed system achieves state-of-the-art camera poses and scene geometry in cluttered dynamic scenarios while running in real time at around 10 FPS.

ADMM-Based Distributed MPC with Control Barrier Functions for Safe Multi-Robot Quadrupedal Locomotion
2026-03-19 cs.RO · math.OC Top: K. Hamed (h=19)
Yicheng Zeng, Ruturaj S. Sambhus, Basit Muhammad Imran, Jeeseop Kim, Vittorio Pastore
Core Contributions
  • Decentralized MPC framework enforcing inter-robot safety via control barrier functions (CBFs), without a central coordinator or global communication network.
  • Node-edge ADMM splitting converts coupled safety constraints into independent quadratic programs solvable in parallel with only neighbor-to-neighbor communication.
  • Symmetric computational load across agents: each robot solves problems of the same size regardless of total fleet size.
  • Integrated in a 3-layer hierarchical controller: distributed trajectory planning → nonlinear MPC → whole-body dynamics — covering the full control stack.
  • 51% reduction in per-cycle planning time vs. centralized MPC in 4-agent experiments on Unitree Go2 hardware, with comparable safety and trajectory quality.
This paper proposes a fully decentralized model predictive control (MPC) framework with control barrier function (CBF) constraints for safety-critical trajectory planning in multi-robot legged systems. The incorporation of CBF constraints introduces explicit inter-agent coupling, which prevents direct decomposition of the resulting optimal control problems. To address this challenge, we reformulate the centralized safety-critical MPC problem using a structured distributed optimization framework based on the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM). By introducing a novel node-edge splitting formulation with consensus constraints, the proposed approach decomposes the global problem into independent node-local and edge-local quadratic programs that can be solved in parallel using only neighbor-to-neighbor communication. This enables fully decentralized trajectory optimization with symmetric computational load across agents while preserving safety and dynamic feasibility.

By defining constraints over tracked 3D keypoints, our method robustly solves diverse multi-agent manipulation tasks from visual observations alone, without relying on training data or environment models.

2026-03-19 cs.RO · cs.AI Top: G. Sartoretti (h=9)
Fengxiaoxiao Li, Xiao Mao, Mingfeng Fan, Yifeng Zhang, Yi Li
Core Contributions
  • Addresses Multi-Objective Multiple Traveling Salesman Problem (MOMTSP) — team routing with competing objectives (total cost vs. makespan) — a combination rarely tackled by learning-based solvers.
  • Conditional encoder fuses preference vectors into instance representations, enabling explicit Pareto front control at inference time without retraining for different trade-off preferences.
  • Collaborative decoder alternates agent selection and node assignment to construct multi-agent tours autoregressively, coordinating the fleet without centralized assignment.
  • Trained on mixed problem-size distributions for generalization across varying numbers of targets, agents, and preference vectors.
  • Outperforms both neural and conventional heuristics on Pareto front approximation quality; validated on a real mobile robot platform.
Robotic systems often require a team of robots to collectively visit multiple targets while optimizing competing objectives, such as total travel cost and makespan. This setting can be formulated as the Multi-Objective Multiple Traveling Salesman Problem (MOMTSP). Although learning-based methods have shown strong performance on the single-agent TSP and multi-objective TSP variants, they rarely address the combined challenges of multi-agent coordination and multi-objective trade-offs. To bridge this gap, we propose CAMO, a conditional neural solver for MOMTSP that generalizes across varying numbers of targets, agents, and preference vectors, and yields high-quality approximations to the Pareto front. Extensive experiments show that CAMO outperforms both neural and conventional heuristics, achieving a closer approximation of Pareto fronts.
