🔭 Research Landscape

If one word ties today's 30 papers together it is "world model", and the batch is unusually reflective about what the term should mean. Wei Zhang's tutorial (#9) tries to impose order, defining world models as action-conditioned predictive models and coining "world action models" as the bridge from predicted futures to control — and the rest of the day reads like a set of instantiations and stress tests of exactly that framing. ABot-M0.5 (#4) argues current World Action Models are structurally mismatched to mobile manipulation because they operate on coarse video chunks and entangle navigation with manipulation; the Structured 4D Latent Predictive Model (#16) attacks the same class from the geometry side, insisting that 2D video world models lack the 3D structure needed for physically consistent planning; and Path Planning in Physically Viable World Models (#29) pushes the idea outdoors, asking what-if terrain-change queries against a reconstruction rather than trusting a stale map. RoboWorld (#22) closes the loop by using video world models not to act but to evaluate policies, confronting head-on the two weaknesses — unreliable rollouts and slow inference — that the tutorial's optimism glosses over.

A second current is the field's growing habit of improving policies without touching their weights. Several of the strongest entries are test-time or adaptation-time interventions layered on frozen backbones: Domain Arithmetic (#12) adapts VLAs across camera-pose and robot-embodiment shifts through one-shot weight-vector arithmetic instead of collecting per-task demos; FAR (#18) turns each deployment failure into contrastive preference data so a robot stops repeating its own mistakes; and DART-VLN (#24) hardens navigation agents purely at inference with memory decay and an anti-loop penalty. AutoSpeed (#2) belongs to the same spirit, extracting stage-adaptive execution speed from demonstrations with no speed or stage labels. The throughline is a bet that generalist policies are bottlenecked less by parameters than by how state, structure, and the learning signal are marshalled at deployment — a theme that echoes recent weeks and again puts the most-cited manipulation authors (Haoqi Yuan's H-Tac tactile pre-training, #1; Wenchao Ding, #2) at the front of the ranking.

The third, quieter theme is honesty about uncertainty and safety in the estimation-and-control backbone, where much of today's methodological rigor lives. Three separate papers reach for conformal prediction to make guarantees distribution-free: robust operational-space control (#10) conformalizes disturbance bounds for redundant manipulators, and Conformalized Distance Fields (#27) conformalizes an entire predicted distance field at once rather than a scalar score, preserving spatial coherence as scenes get crowded. FastBridge (#15) makes the same move for aerial safety, replacing the fiction that commanded accelerations are instantaneous with an actuator-aware barrier enforced through full quadrotor dynamics, while GPU-parallel linearization bounds (#11) certify error for neural-network dynamics so learned models can enter real-time robust control. Autonomous driving forms its own reliability sub-plot — occlusion-aware planning via VLMs (#5), metric-agnostic forecasting that refuses to game benchmark metrics (#17), uncertainty-explicit V2X occupancy grids (#26), and a strategic guide (#28) that reframes dataset creation as a targeted intervention rather than default scale. Taken together the day reads as a field trying to make its most ambitious idea — predictive world models — actually trustworthy, whether through cleaner definitions, weight-free adaptation, or conformal safety.

Human-Centric Transferable Tactile Pre-Training for Dexterous Robotic Manipulation

2026-07-01 cs.ROcs.CV 👤 Haoqi Yuan (h=12)
Chi Zhang, Penglin Cai, Ziheng Xi, Haoqi Yuan, Hao Luo
Core Contributions
  • Attacks the tactile-data bottleneck from the human side: builds H-Tac, a 160-hour egocentric human-video tactile-action dataset, sidestepping the small, narrow-contact robot datasets that cap tactile VLA performance.
  • Reframes tactile learning as human-to-robot transfer, pre-training contact representations from how people touch and manipulate rather than from dynamics-agnostic post-training on scarce robot tactile logs.
  • Targets the specific ceiling of tactile VLA: existing models bolt tactile on without modeling contact dynamics, whereas H-Tac aims to instill a dynamics-aware tactile prior that raises the downstream performance ceiling.
  • Positions egocentric human video as a cheap, scalable substitute for hardware-limited tactile collection — a bet that human demonstration data transfers usefully to robot hands.
Show abstract
As an essential modality for dexterous and contact-rich tasks, tactile sensing provides precise force feedback that cannot be reliably inferred from vision. However, limited by hardware and data collection systems, existing datasets with tactility remain small in scale and narrow in contact coverage. Meanwhile, Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models with tactile modality are constrained on dynamics-agnostic post-training, which limits the performance ceiling on downstream tasks. In this paper, we present H-Tac, a large-scale tactile-action dataset with 160-hour egocentric human videos containing rich and diverse contact interactions, together with a tactile pre-training framework that learns transferable, dynamics-aware tactile representations and transfers them to dexterous robotic manipulation.

ABot-M0.5: Unified Mobility-and-Manipulation World Action Model

2026-07-01 cs.CVcs.RO 👤 Xinyuan Chang (h=8)
Ronghan Chen, Yandan Yang, Zuojin Tang, Dongjie Huo, Tong Lin
Core Contributions
  • Argues existing World Action Models are structurally mismatched to mobile manipulation: they operate on coarse video chunks and model navigation and manipulation as one entangled action stream, missing fine contact dynamics.
  • Unifies mobility and manipulation in a single world action model while disentangling the two action modes, directly countering the action-distribution conflicts that plague jointly-modeled navigation-plus-manipulation.
  • Fixes a training/inference mismatch where inverse dynamics is supervised in a way that does not match autoregressive rollout, a known source of compounding error at deployment.
  • Aims squarely at general-purpose robots that must both navigate and manipulate — a regime handled poorly by reactive VLAs (no world model) and by coarse video-chunk WAMs alike.
Show abstract
Mobile manipulation is a key capability for general-purpose robots, yet remains challenging for current embodied learning methods. VLA policies are typically reactive and lack explicit world modeling, while existing World Action Models (WAMs) are still poorly aligned with the structure of mobile manipulation: they operate on coarse video chunks, model entangled navigation-manipulation actions, and train inverse dynamics under supervision that does not match autoregressive inference. As a result, they often miss fine-grained contact dynamics, suffer from action-distribution conflicts, and accumulate error over long horizons. We present ABot-M0.5, a unified world action model for mobile manipulation that disentangles navigation and manipulation and aligns training with autoregressive inference.

Domain Arithmetic: One-Shot VLA Adaptation under Environmental Shifts

2026-07-01 cs.ROcs.CVcs.LG 👤 Donghyun Shin (h=1)
Taewook Kang, Taeheon Kim, Donghyun Shin, Jonghyun Choi
Core Contributions
  • Attacks a common VLA failure: the same learned task breaks under environmental shifts such as a changed camera pose or a swap from a Panda to a UR5e arm.
  • Proposes one-shot adaptation via weight-vector arithmetic — adding a domain-specific information direction in weight space — instead of collecting multiple demonstrations per task in the target domain.
  • Analogy-based framing treats domain shift as a composable vector, so adaptation reuses structure rather than retraining, cutting the data-curation and training burden.
  • Aims to make VLA models portable across robots and viewpoints from minimal demonstrations, a practical lever for real deployment where re-collecting data is expensive.
Show abstract
Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models often fail to perform the same learned tasks under environmental shifts, such as changes in camera pose and shifts to a different but similar robot (e.g., from Panda to UR5e). Adapting these models to the shifted environment (i.e., target domain) often requires training on multiple demonstrations for each task, which are costly to collect. To reduce the burden of data curation and training, we propose an analogy-based method that adapts VLA models under environmental shifts through weight vector arithmetic with domain-specific information addition, named Domain Arithmetic. Our method enables one-shot adaptation to new domains by composing domain-specific weight directions, substantially reducing the demonstrations and training required.

AutoSpeed: Annotation-Free Stage-Adaptive Motion Speed Learning for Robot Manipulation

2026-07-01 cs.RO 👤 Wenchao Ding (h=10)
Qingda Hu, Ziheng Qiu, Jieru Zhao, Zhongxue Gan, Wenchao Ding
Core Contributions
  • Challenges a hidden assumption in imitation-learned visuomotor policies: they copy expert execution speed and use a fixed prediction horizon, so easy and hard stages of a task run at the same pace.
  • Learns stage-adaptive motion speeds and prediction horizons — slowing for precise contact phases, accelerating through easy transit — to raise overall task throughput.
  • Key practicality: requires no speed or stage annotations, inferring stage difficulty from the demonstrations themselves, so it drops onto existing policies model-agnostically.
  • The payoff is faster task completion without retraining the base policy, treating temporal allocation as a free lever most IL policies leave on the table.
Show abstract
Different stages of manipulation tasks exhibit varying levels of difficulty, suggesting stage-dependent motion speeds and temporal prediction horizons. However, existing IL-based visuomotor policies typically imitate the execution speed of expert demonstrations and operate with a fixed temporal prediction horizon, limiting flexibility and overall task throughput. In this paper, we introduce AutoSpeed, a model-agnostic learning framework that enables existing visuomotor policies to predict trajectories with stage-adaptive motion speeds, without requiring speed or stage annotations. We treat future prediction horizons as adaptable and infer stage structure directly from demonstrations, yielding faster task completion while preserving success.

Learning from Demonstration via Spatiotemporal Tubes for Unknown Euler-Lagrange Systems

2026-07-01 cs.ROeess.SY 👤 Ratnangshu Das (h=6)
Ratnangshu Das, Puneeth Shankar, Varuni Buereddy, Ravi Prakash, Pushpak Jagtap
Core Contributions
  • Reframes demonstrations not as a fixed reference to track but as a data-driven safety specification — an envelope of acceptable behavior rather than one trajectory to imitate.
  • Learns Spatiotemporal Tubes via heteroscedastic Gaussian Processes so the tube tightens where the task demands precision and widens where it tolerates slack, capturing time-varying intent.
  • Provides a closed-form feedback controller that enforces the learned tube while respecting actuator limits, for unknown Euler-Lagrange systems with no explicit dynamics model required.
  • Unifies motion learning and control in one framework, unlike decoupled LfD pipelines that first learn a path and then separately track it.
Show abstract
We present STT-LfD, a unified Learning from Demonstration (LfD) framework that integrates motion learning with control for unknown Euler-Lagrange systems. Unlike traditional decoupled approaches that track a fixed reference, the proposed method treats demonstrations as a data-driven safety specification. Using heteroscedastic Gaussian Processes, STT-LfD learns Spatiotemporal Tubes (STTs) as an intent envelope that capture time-varying precision requirements of a task. A closed-form feedback controller then enforces these learned constraints while respecting actuator limits, without requiring an explicit dynamics model of the system.

VLM-AR3L: Vision-Language Models for Absolute and Relative Rewards in Reinforcement Learning

2026-07-01 cs.RO 👤 Min-Chun Hu (h=5)
Kuan-Chen Chen, Winston Chen, Wei-Fang Sun, Min-Chun Hu
Core Contributions
  • Tackles reward design in open-ended RL where goals are abstract and hard to quantify, using VLMs to supply the reward signal from visual observations plus a language goal.
  • Distinctive twist: learns both absolute rewards (a scalar value for a single state) and relative rewards (a preference between two states), rather than committing to one reward type.
  • Trains both reward models from VLM-generated preference labels, letting a pretrained VLM's judgment stand in for hand-engineered reward functions.
  • Targets tasks where scalar rewards alone are brittle, combining absolute grounding with relative comparisons for a more stable and informative learning signal.
Show abstract
Designing effective reward functions remains a major challenge in reinforcement learning (RL), particularly in open-ended environments where task goals are abstract and difficult to quantify. In this work, we present VLM-AR3L, a framework that leverages Vision-Language Models (VLMs) to provide both absolute and relative rewards for RL. VLM-AR3L interprets an agent's visual observations in the context of a natural language task goal, and learns both absolute and relative rewards from VLM-generated preference labels. The absolute reward model predicts scalar evaluations for individual states, while the relative reward model captures preferences between state pairs, together providing a richer supervisory signal.

2026-07-01 cs.ROcs.AIcs.LG 👤 Haoran Hao (h=0)
Haoran Hao, Shahram Najam Syed, Jeffrey Ichnowski, Jeff Schneider
Core Contributions
  • Attacks naive retries that repeat the same mistake, and recovery methods that lean on human intervention, by letting robots learn from their own failures at test time.
  • Uses Failure-Contrastive Preference Adaptation, building preference data from failed attempts to steer the policy away from behaviors that just failed.
  • Adds lightweight test-time action adjustment so the robot adapts online and eventually completes the task autonomously, with no human in the loop.
  • Frames failure as a learning signal for continual policy improvement, turning each deployment failure into training data rather than a dead end.
Show abstract
Robot policies inevitably encounter failures when deployed in real environments. Naive retries often repeat the same mistakes, while many existing recovery methods rely on human intervention. In this paper, we propose Failure-Aware Retry (FAR), a framework that enables robots to learn from previous failures at test time, adapt their behavior accordingly, and eventually complete the task autonomously. FAR combines Failure-Contrastive Preference Adaptation, which constructs preference learning data from failures to steer the policy away from previously unsuccessful behaviors, with lightweight action adjustment at test time for continual improvement.

Core Contributions

Depth-only 3D geometry provides a privacy-preserving alternative, but the absence of appearance-based semantic cues makes open-vocabulary predictions highly uncertain and less reliable. Under this setting, we propose to convert uncertainty into a guidance signal for test-time optimization, refining depth-only open-vocabulary predictions without exposing appearance information.

2026-07-01 cs.RO 👤 Suraj Borate (h=0)
Suraj Borate, Aarav Shah, Madhu Vadali
Core Contributions
  • Reframes GPS-denied indoor localization as semantic reasoning rather than geometric estimation, mimicking how humans localize from object-level cues and labeled maps.
  • Asks a VLM to infer pose from three inputs at once — a front camera image, a polar LiDAR scan, and a top-down semantic grid map — fusing modalities inside one model.

Show abstract

We fine-tune Qwen2.5-VL-7B with LoRA and attach a lightweight regression head that predicts continuous pose coordinates (x, y, theta) directly from the final hidden state, bypassing text generation. Training uses a composite objective combining pose regression with semantic grounding.

DART-VLN: Test-Time Memory Decay and Anti-Loop Regularization for Discrete Vision-Language Navigation

2026-07-01 cs.ROcs.AI 👤 Shaoheng Zhang (h=0)
Shaoheng Zhang, Zhichen Li, Jie Mei
Core Contributions
  • Improves discrete vision-language navigation at test time only, with no retraining, targeting two failure modes of memory-based agents acting under partial observability.
  • Test-Time Memory Decay reweights stored memory at readout to suppress stale, redundant evidence — without rewriting the stored content itself.
  • Anti-Loop Regularization adds a lightweight next-hop penalty to curb inefficient local backtracking, a common cause of VLN agents getting stuck in loops.
  • Both are training-free control add-ons for frozen backbones, hardening existing VLN agents cheaply instead of retraining them.
Show abstract
Memory-based discrete vision-language navigation (VLN) agents must act under partial observability, yet even strong frozen backbones remain vulnerable at test time. Two common failure modes are stale historical evidence at memory readout and inefficient local backtracking during action selection. We present DART-VLN, a training-free test-time control framework for discrete VLN. DART-VLN combines Test-Time Memory Decay, a read-side memory reweighting rule that suppresses stale and redundant evidence without rewriting stored content, with Anti-Loop Regularization, a lightweight next-hop penalty that discourages repetitive backtracking.

2026-07-01 cs.ROcs.AIcs.CV 👤 Sangjae Bae (h=8)
Amirhosein Chahe, Tyler Naes, Jovin D'sa, Faizan M. Tariq, Sangjae Bae
Core Contributions
  • Rejects uniform conservatism toward occlusions in AV planning — which produces needlessly defensive driving — and instead asks which specific hidden agents actually matter to the plan.
  • Uses VLMs to reason about the particular occluded agents most critical to the ego trajectory, bridging perception and planning rather than blindly inferring hidden space.
  • Ranks occlusions by Planning KL-divergence (PKL), quantifying each hidden agent's impact on the planner so attention concentrates on planning-critical unknowns.

Show abstract

This work bridges the critical gap between perception and planning by enabling Vision-Language Models (VLMs) to identify and reason about the specific hidden agents that are most critical to the ego-vehicle's trajectory. We introduce a novel framework that uses Planning KL-divergence (PKL), an importance measure that ranks occluded regions by their effect on the ego plan.
