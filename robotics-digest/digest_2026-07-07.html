🔭 Research Landscape

If today's 30 papers share a headline, it's that VLA acceleration is shifting from bigger pretraining to cheap post-hoc interventions. OTQL (#1) fine-tunes flow policies with just 50-60 interaction episodes, pushing single-task success from 36% to 86% while cutting inference steps by 70%; ActionCache (#12) gets up to 34.43x speedup on GR00T-N1.6 with zero retraining at all, just a reusable cache of past denoising states; and SIEVE (#17) shows a policy can match full-data training using only 50% of demonstrations once redundant trajectories are pruned by compositional structure rather than scored individually. These three attack the same VLA cost problem — slow, data-hungry policies — from three different angles (RL fine-tuning, inference caching, data curation) without any of them retraining a foundation model from scratch, a notable contrast to LingBot-VLA 2.0 (#15), which takes the opposite bet and scales to 60,000 hours of cross-embodiment pretraining data.

A second, closely related thread is explicit geometric grounding returning to problems that had drifted toward flat 2D or latent representations. Lift3D-VLA (#14) aligns raw 3D point clouds with pretrained 2D positional embeddings so a VLA's vision encoder can reason about geometry and future dynamics jointly, gaining 10-11 points of success on MetaWorld/RLBench specifically under out-of-distribution perturbations. EAGOR (#2) makes the analogous move for omnidirectional navigation, replacing equirectangular-projection reasoning with a spherical-harmonic belief field to eliminate seam and latitude distortion. UniLM-Nav (#11) grounds affordance points and lifts them into a robot-centric frame for last-mile navigation. The common wager across all three: representations that respect the underlying geometry generalize better than ones that treat images as flat 2D arrays, and the OOD-specific gains in #14 are direct evidence for it.

A third thread, less about new capability and more about intellectual honesty, runs through this batch's diagnostic papers. "Why does Deep Learning Improve Visual SLAM?" (#9) isolates learned 2D data association and uncertainty — not the recurrent architecture — as the actual source of DL-SLAM's advantage. "Imagined Rollouts are Kinematic, Not Dynamic" (#24) shows a DreamerV3 world model's imagination error stays flat across a friction sweep even as the real policy's reward collapses through a gait-collapse boundary, meaning the model's imagined rollouts never "notice" the physics changing. The semantic-handoff paper (#18) quantifies how skills hitting 77-100% success in isolation still stall when chained from real prior-skill end states. And the fisheye calibration paper (#22) traces persistent calibration failures to a specific ill-conditioning mechanism rather than detector quality, lifting success from 68.1% to 99.3% with smarter frame selection alone. None of these four propose a new architecture; all four instead give the field a sharper, falsifiable account of why existing systems fail, which is arguably more useful right now than another benchmark-topping method.

Optimal Transport Q-Learning for Flow Policy Steering and Acceleration

2026-07-07 cs.RO 👤 S. Vijayakumar (h=50)
Andreas Sochopoulos, Esmeralda S. Whitammer, Nikolaos Tsagkas, João Moura, Michael Gienger
Core Contributions
  • RL post-training approach for flow/diffusion policies via advantage-weighted conditional optimal transport flow matching, explicitly avoiding the expensive distillation step that's become a default requirement for accelerating flow-based policies.
  • Needs only 50-60 interaction episodes to retrain, framing acceleration as a cheap post-training problem rather than a full retrain from a large offline dataset.
  • Quant: single-task success rises 36% to 86%, a pretrained VLA's success rises 38% to 76%, while inference steps per action drop 70% — fixing suboptimality and latency in the same pass rather than trading one for the other.
  • Suggests the robot's own rollout experience, not more human demonstrations, is what closes both the performance and speed gaps in flow-based VLA pipelines.
Show abstract
Diffusion and flow policies have recently demonstrated remarkable performance in robotic applications by accurately capturing multimodal robot trajectory distributions, especially in the context of vision language action (VLA) models. However, high quality policy performance also requires fast inference and high quality demonstrations, which are often hard to get. Lack of these leads to suboptimal policy behaviors and failure under distribution shifts. In this work we address the problem of fine-tuning and accelerating suboptimal flow-based policies using the robot's experience through RL post-training. We introduce Optimal Transport Q-Learning (OTQL), a new method for finetuning flow policies using advantage weighted conditional optimal transport flow matching. OTQL can finetune and accelerate flows with an interaction budget of 50-60 episodes while avoiding computationally expensive distillation in simulation and real-world robot tasks. Our results show that OTQL post-trains flow policies using the robot's own experience, increasing average success percentage of single-task policies from 36% to 86% and of a pre-trained VLA from 38% to 76% while reducing the number of inference steps per action generation by 70%.
#12 h=10

Training-Free Acceleration for Vision-Language-Action Models with Action Caching and Refinement

2026-07-07 cs.ROcs.CVcs.LG 👤 Daichi Fujiki (h=10)
Ryuji Oi, Hikari Otsuka, Kosuke Matsushima, Yuki Ichikawa, Masato Motomura
Core Contributions
  • Plug-and-play, training-free cache for flow-matching VLA action heads: stores intermediate denoising states keyed by compact multimodal context and reuses them across episodes or even different tasks to warm-start generation instead of denoising from scratch.
  • No retraining or architecture change required — directly deployable on existing flow-based VLA checkpoints, unlike distillation-based acceleration that needs retraining per model.
  • Quant: up to 11.75x speedup on π0.5 and 34.43x on GR00T-N1.6 while maintaining high success in a low-latency regime — the large gap between the two suggests cache hit-rate depends heavily on how the action head represents its denoising trajectory.
  • Directly complements #1: where OTQL reduces the number of required denoising steps via RL, ActionCache reduces the cost of each step via reuse — two compatible attacks on the same VLA inference bottleneck.
Show abstract
Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models have emerged as a promising approach for generalizable robotic manipulations. In particular, flow matching-based VLA models have shown remarkable success due to their capability to generate precise and smooth action sequences and capture multimodal distributions. However, the iterative denoising process in the action head acts as a major computational bottleneck, posing a critical challenge for real-time deployment. To address this challenge, we propose ActionCache, a plug-and-play external cache that opportunistically reuses past intermediate actions to warm-start generations from the vicinity of target actions, thereby drastically reducing the inference latency. Specifically, ActionCache stores the intermediate actions with compact multimodal keys, which enables retrieval from similar past contexts across different episodes or even different tasks. Experimental results in simulation and real-world environments demonstrate that ActionCache maintains high task success rates in a low-latency regime, achieving inference acceleration of up to $11.75\times$ and $34.43\times$ for representative flow-based VLA models, $π_{0.5}$ and GR00T-N1.6, respectively.

Lift3D-VLA: Lifting VLA Models to 3D Geometry and Dynamics-Aware Manipulation

2026-07-07 cs.ROcs.CV 👤 Chenyang Gu (h=9)
Jiaming Liu, Qingpo Wuwu, Nuowei Han, Hao Chen, Zhuoyang Liu
Core Contributions
  • Directly addresses geometric information loss in existing 3D-VLA encoders by aligning raw point clouds with pretrained 2D positional embeddings, allowing point-cloud encoding inside the same vision encoder used for 2D pretraining rather than a bolted-on 3D branch.
  • Introduces Geometry-Centric Masked Autoencoding, a dual-objective pretext task that reconstructs the current point cloud and predicts its future geometric evolution — teaching the encoder structure and physical dynamics from one self-supervised signal.
  • Layer-wise temporal action modeling spreads action-chunk prediction across multiple LLM layers for temporal coherence, rather than predicting the whole chunk from a single final layer.
  • Quant: +10.8%/+11.1% mean success over the best prior VLA on MetaWorld/RLBench, +4pp over the strongest real-world baseline, with stronger OOD-perturbation generalization — geometric grounding specifically helps distribution shift, not just in-distribution accuracy.
Show abstract
Recently, Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models have demonstrated strong generalization across diverse tasks. However, effective robotic manipulation in physical environments fundamentally requires geometric understanding and spatial reasoning. While some VLA approaches attempt to incorporate 3D information, they are constrained by limited data availability and geometric information loss in current 3D encoding pipelines, and fail to jointly capture 3D geometry and temporally structured actions in dynamic environments. To address these limitations, we introduce Lift3D-VLA, a unified VLA framework that equips models with explicit 3D point cloud reasoning and enables temporally coherent action generation. First, building upon our previous work Lift3D, an enhanced 2D model-lifting strategy is proposed to geometrically align 3D points with pretrained 2D positional embeddings. This design enables direct point-cloud encoding within the VLA vision encoder while minimizing spatial information loss. Based on explicit 3D inputs, we propose Geometry-Centric Masked Autoencoding (GC-MAE), a dual-objective self-supervised framework that reconstructs the current point cloud while predicting its future geometric evolution. This formulation allows the 2D vision encoder to internalize both 3D structure and physical dynamics. To fully exploit 3D representations, we further design layer-wise temporal action modeling, which leverages multiple layers of the LLM to collaboratively predict action chunks, enabling temporally consistent predictions. Across 22 simulated tasks and 8 real-world manipulation tasks, Lift3D-VLA achieves 10.8% and 11.1% higher mean success rates on MetaWorld and RLBench than the best-performing prior VLA methods, and outperforms the strongest real-world baseline by 4 percentage points, while exhibiting stronger generalization to out-of-distribution perturbations.

From Foundation to Application: Improving VLA Models in Practice

2026-07-07 cs.RO 👤 Shuailei Ma (h=9)
Wei Wu, Fangjing Wang, Fan Lu, He Sun, Shi Liu
Core Contributions
  • Scales pretraining to ~60,000 hours (50K robot trajectories across 20 embodiments plus 10K egocentric human videos) specifically to test whether cross-embodiment coverage, not just raw data volume, drives generalization.
  • Expands the action space beyond dual-arm platforms to heads, waists, mobile bases, and dexterous hands, pushing VLA scope from tabletop manipulation toward whole-body mobile manipulation.
  • Uses future prediction as an auxiliary proxy task, fed by separate video-representation and depth-estimation priors, rather than training temporal understanding purely from the action-prediction loss.
  • Demonstrates cross-embodiment long-horizon mobile manipulation across two distinct robot platforms on the GM-100 generalist benchmark — the payoff is transfer across embodiments, not just a benchmark score on one.
Show abstract
Despite recent progress of VLA foundation models, the disparity between laboratory conditions and real-world applications continues to impede their practical implementation. To bridge this gap, we present LingBot-VLA 2.0, which advances LingBot-VLA through improvements in three functional domains. (1) Generalization across tasks and embodiments. Compared to the previous version, we revamp the data processing pipeline and curate around 60,000 hours of data for pretraining, including 50,000 hours of robot trajectories spanning 20 robot configurations and 10,000 hours of egocentric human videos. (2) Expanded action space in addition to dual-arm hardware platforms. In particular, our system accommodates degrees of freedom for the heads, waists, mobile bases, and dexterous hands, thereby empowering the robots to tackle more complex tasks in practical scenarios. (3) Predictive dynamics modeling for improved temporal reasoning. Specifically, we formulate future prediction as a proxy task, facilitated by a video representation model for semantic priors and a depth estimation model for geometric cues. Evaluations on the GM-100 benchmark, conducted in a generalist setting, validate the beneficial impact of these proposed modifications. Furthermore, benefiting from the expanded pretraining data that covers whole-body degrees of freedom, LingBot-VLA-2.0 demonstrates strong cross-embodiment long-horizon mobile manipulation capability across the two robotic platforms.

SIEVE: Structure-Aware Data Selection for Imitation Learning with VLA Models

2026-07-07 cs.RO 👤 Shijie Lian (h=7)
Changti Wu, Bin Yu, Zhaolong Shen, Shijie Lian, Xiaopeng Lin
Core Contributions
  • Reframes imitation-learning data selection around reusable motion primitives and their transition interfaces, rather than scoring whole trajectories or individual state-action pairs — capturing compositional structure that flat scoring methods miss.
  • Allocates a selection budget across composition patterns under a diminishing-returns objective, then picks medoid (central, stable) trajectories within each bucket — an explicit anti-redundancy mechanism rather than simple top-k scoring.
  • Quant: matches or surpasses full-data training using only 50% of demonstrations and 50% of training steps — redundant data isn't just wasteful, it can be actively pruned without hurting the policy.
  • Cross-cutting with #15's data-scaling story: SIEVE argues coverage of reusable structure, not raw hours, is the real scaling lever for VLA imitation learning.
Show abstract
Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models are typically trained by imitation learning on large-scale robot demonstration datasets, but more data does not necessarily yield better policies due to redundancy, noise, and uneven coverage. Existing data selection methods often assess demonstrations at either the trajectory or state-action level, missing the reusable structures that compose long-horizon behaviors. In this paper, we propose SIEVE, a structure-aware data selection method for VLA imitation learning. SIEVE views demonstrations as compositions of reusable primitives and transition interfaces. It first discovers visuo-motor primitives from segmented trajectories, then allocates selection budgets to composition patterns by maximizing reuse-aware structural exposure under diminishing returns. Finally, it selects medoid trajectories within each composition-pattern bucket to retain central, stable, and imitation-friendly demonstrations. Experiments across multiple datasets, benchmarks, and VLA models show that SIEVE consistently outperforms competitive data selection baselines. Notably, SIEVE can surpass full-data training while using only 50% of demonstrations and 50% of training steps, suggesting that reusable structure, captured through primitives and transitions, is an important signal for efficient VLA imitation learning.

Diagnosing Semantic Handoff Failures in Agent-Orchestrated Vision-Language-Action Skill Composition

2026-07-07 cs.RO 👤 Weitao Zhou (h=7)
Ke Rui, Yushen Zuo, Jiawei Wang, Haoran Jia, Jinming Ma
Core Contributions
  • Distinguishes isolated skill competence (measured from clean, hand-designed skill-boundary states) from compositional robustness (measured from real chained states a prior skill actually produced) — a distinction most long-horizon VLA benchmarks collapse into a single end-to-end number.
  • Uses multi-view VLM verification as an agent orchestrator deciding advance/retry/replan between π0.5-based skill checkpoints, rather than assuming skills chain automatically once each is competent alone.
  • Quant: individual skills hit 77-100% success from clean snapshots, yet composed rollouts stall frequently from chained states — quantifying exactly how much the clean-boundary assumption inflates apparent competence.
  • Turns near-zero end-to-end success into categorized diagnostics (next-skill readiness, target grounding, low-level control failures) instead of one opaque failure rate, giving concrete targets for future skill-library design.
Show abstract
Long-horizon household tasks require robots to compose many language-conditioned skills, yet the boundary between consecutive skills is rarely explicit. A skill may satisfy its own postcondition while leaving the robot, objects, or camera views in a state from which the next skill cannot reliably start. We study this semantic handoff problem in BEHAVIOR-1K through an agent-orchestrated vision-language-action execution harness. The harness invokes $π_{0.5}$-based skill checkpoints trained from cleaned BEHAVIOR-1K demonstrations, assigns each skill typed arguments and a step budget, and uses multi-view vision-language model verification to decide whether execution should advance, retry, or replan. To separate isolated skill competence from long-horizon compositional robustness, we evaluate the same checkpoints under two initial-state distributions: clean skill-boundary snapshots and chained terminal states produced by previous skills. Selected navigation, grasping, placement, and door-opening skills achieve 77--100% success from clean snapshots under human-reviewed verification, yet composed rollouts still frequently stall from chained states. Execution traces attribute these failures to next-skill readiness, target grounding, and low-level control execution, revealing a substantial gap between single-skill success and reliable long-horizon task completion. These findings turn near-zero end-to-end task success into actionable diagnostics, showing that future VLA skill libraries must learn robustness to the messy chained-state distribution that clean demonstrations systematically underrepresent.

FORGE: Towards Functional Tool-Use Generalization via Keypoint Trajectory Reasoning

2026-07-07 cs.ROcs.AIcs.CV 👤 Animesh Garg (h=7)
Chuhao Zhou, Liquan Wang, Shuxin Cao, Xiangyu Chen, Yuxuan Hu
Core Contributions
  • Formalizes "functional generalization" — using a novel tool to accomplish the same function as a trained one — as distinct from ordinary generalization, since visually/functionally similar tools still demand entirely different motor patterns.
  • Empirically compares affordance images, human video prompts, and 2D keypoint trajectories as intermediate representations, finding keypoint trajectories uniquely balance functional expressiveness against groundability into robot actions.
  • A two-stage decoupling — predict generalizable keypoint trajectories from action-free data, then ground them into robot actions from limited demonstrations — separates the data-hungry reasoning step from the data-cheap grounding step.
  • Quant: over 2x average success-rate improvement over SOTA on unseen tools across a seven-tool hitting-function benchmark, in both simulation and the real world.
Show abstract
While humans readily repurpose a book, a stone, or a shoe to drive a nail, robots trained on specific tools fail to transfer the same function to novel ones -- a gap we formalize as functional generalization. Such tools share a common functional intent that is visually recognizable, yet this perceptual similarity does not carry over to action space, where each tool demands an entirely different motor pattern. To bridge this gap, we explore intermediate representations including affordance images, human video prompts, and 2D keypoint trajectories, finding that keypoint trajectories best balance functional expressiveness and action groundability. Building on this, we propose FunctiOnal Reasoning and Grounded Execution (FORGE), a two-stage policy that decouples functional reasoning from action execution: predicting generalizable keypoint trajectories from action-free data, then grounding them into robot actions with limited demonstrations. On a seven-tool hitting-function benchmark, FORGE consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods on unseen tools in both simulation and the real world, achieving over 2X improvement in average success rate.

Image2Sim: Scaling Embodied Navigation via Generative Neural Simulator

2026-07-07 cs.CVcs.RO 👤 Yinghao Xu (h=35)
Zihan Wang, Seungjun Lee, Yinghao Xu, Gim Hee Lee
Core Contributions
  • Decouples 3D spatial anchoring (feed-forward feature-Gaussian lifting) from photorealistic rendering (a separate one-step pixel-flow model) instead of one monolithic neural renderer — the split is what turns noisy, sparse Gaussian projections into clean panoramic RGB-D.
  • Functions as a fully automated data engine: converts ordinary video/image collections, not curated scans, into interactive scenes at a scale (~20K scenes, 10M+ navigation samples) manual capture can't approach.
  • Directly targets the classic scan-realism vs. synthetic-scalability tradeoff, arguing neural simulation from casual footage can deliver both rather than forcing a choice.
  • Reports zero-shot real-world transfer from policies trained purely in the neural simulation — the training substrate need not be a real or hand-built simulator at all.
Show abstract
Embodied navigation aims to build agents that interpret multimodal goals, reason in 3D space, and reach target destinations reliably in the real world. However, progress remains constrained by the lack of scalable, high-fidelity, and physically grounded interactive environments. Although real-world scanned datasets offer visual realism, they are limited by scale. In contrast, synthetic simulators scale more easily but often exhibit large sim-to-real gaps. We introduce Image2Sim, a real-time neural simulation framework that constructs high-quality interactive environments from posed RGB-D image sequences. The central idea is to decouple 3D spatial anchoring from photorealistic observation synthesis. For scene construction, Image2Sim uses a feed-forward feature Gaussian model that lifts posed RGB-D observations into a 3D feature-Gaussian representation in a single pass. For rendering, we propose a Geometry-Aware One-Step Pixel Flow model that transforms sparse and noisy Gaussian projections into high-quality panoramic RGB-D observations. Image2Sim also serves as a fully automated embodied data engine that generates high-fidelity observations, executable actions, and diverse navigation instructions at scale. It converts large collections of videos and images into nearly 20K interactive scenes and synthesizes more than 10 million navigation training samples. Navigation models trained entirely in these neural environments achieve strong improvements on major benchmarks and transfer effectively to real-world zero-shot settings. These results suggest that scalable neural simulation can serve as a practical training substrate for embodied navigation at scale.

RoboTALES: Learning Reasoning-Guided Robot Policies via Task-Aligned Simulated Futures

2026-07-07 cs.RO 👤 Nicklas Hansen (h=19)
Hanan Gani, Tejal Kulkarni, Madhoolika Chodavarapu, Nicklas Hansen, Manmohan Chandraker
Core Contributions
  • Targets a specific failure of using video-generation models for policy learning: imagined futures drift from task intent and aren't reliably action-conditional, making raw generator rollouts unreliable as planning substrates.
  • Anchors generation with a hierarchical LLM planner that decomposes tasks into subgoals plus a VLM critic providing reward feedback — language-level structure constrains what would otherwise be unconstrained pixel generation.
  • Trains in a single stage where reasoning directly guides imagination, distinct from typical two-stage generate-then-distill pipelines.
  • Reports consistent gains over existing methods on RoboCasa/LIBERO10, with the largest margins specifically on long-horizon tasks — the reasoning anchor mainly pays off where compounding error is worst.
Show abstract
Pretrained video generative models are promising backbones for visuomotor control, but their imagined futures often drift from task intent and are not reliably action-conditional. As a result, these models can be difficult to use for planning or policy extraction. To address these limitations, we propose RoboTALES, a single-stage framework that learns task-aligned simulated futures and uses them to train robot policies. Our approach introduces two key innovations: (1) a hierarchical LLM-based planner that breaks complex tasks into a sequence of subgoals to guide the model's imagination; and (2) a VLM-based critic that evaluates these ``imagined'' futures and uses reward-based feedback to keep the model's internal representations focused on the goal. By anchoring the video generator in abstract reasoning, we produce temporally consistent rollouts and more coherent actions. We evaluate RoboTALES on diverse manipulation tasks from RoboCasa and LIBERO10, and show that our method consistently outperforms existing methods, especially in long-horizon tasks. Our code and models are publicly available at https://github.com/hananshafi/RoboTALES.

RynnWorld-Teleop: An Action-Conditioned World Model for Digital Teleoperation

2026-07-07 cs.RO 👤 Biao Gong (h=6)
Haoyu Zhao, Xingyue Zhao, Hangyu Li, Biao Gong, Kehan Li
Core Contributions
  • Introduces "digital teleoperation": replaces the physical robot entirely with a generative world model during data collection, so an operator's hand-pose stream, not real hardware, drives synthesis of egocentric video — decoupling data collection from needing a functioning robot at all.
  • The recorded pose stream is embodiment-agnostic and retargetable to any target robot afterward, meaning one teleoperation session can seed training data for multiple different robot bodies.
  • Streaming autoregressive distillation compresses generation to single-pass inference, hitting 40+ FPS real-time on a single H100 — a substantial systems contribution, since naive video-diffusion generation would be far too slow for interactive teleoperation.
  • Reports policies trained purely on generated data achieve zero-shot sim2real transfer, and mixing generated data into real datasets consistently improves success — positioning this as a practical data-augmentation engine, not just a generation demo.
Show abstract
Scaling robot learning requires massive, diverse trajectory data, yet collection is currently bottlenecked by physical teleoperation, where every demonstration binds operator time to specific hardware and workspaces. We introduce digital teleoperation, a paradigm that decouples data collection from physical constraints by replacing the real robot with a generative world model. In this framework, an operator's hand-pose stream drives a robot-centric generative world model to synthesize high-fidelity egocentric videos from a single reference image. The recorded pose stream serves as an embodiment-agnostic action label transferable to any target robot via standard retargeting, yielding complete state-action trajectories for imitation learning independent of physical hardware. We instantiate this paradigm in RynnWorld-Teleop, a system that integrates depth-aware skeletal conditioning, progressive human-to-robot training on a video Diffusion Transformer, and streaming autoregressive distillation. This pipeline compresses the generative process into a single-pass inference, enabling 40+ FPS, real-time interactive generation on a single H100 GPU. Policies trained exclusively on RynnWorld-Teleop-generated data achieve effective zero-shot Sim2Real transfer across dexterous and diverse bimanual tasks. Moreover, augmenting real-world datasets with our digitally teleoperated data consistently improves success rates, demonstrating that RynnWorld-Teleop serves as a high-fidelity, scalable data engine for the next generation of robotic agents.

Imagined Rollouts are Kinematic, Not Dynamic: A Diagnosis of Long-Horizon World-Model Failure

2026-07-07 cs.RO 👤 Sebastian Schmidt (h=5)
Finn Rasmus Schäfer, Korbinian Moller, Yuan Gao, Christian Oefinger, Sebastian Schmidt
Core Contributions
  • Reframes "compounding error" in long-horizon world models — the standard generic explanation for planning failure — into a specific, testable claim: models imagine kinematically (following a closed-form kinematic null) rather than dynamically (respecting real physical forces).
  • Introduces the imagined Kinematic-Consistency Error (iKCE), a per-step diagnostic comparing rollouts to that kinematic null, plus a perturbation protocol testing whether imagination responds when physics actually change regime.
  • Key finding: on a DreamerV3 checkpoint, imagined iKCE runs roughly 100x above matched real-physics rollouts, and stays statistically flat across a friction sweep even as the trained policy's actual reward collapses through a gait-collapse boundary — the world model doesn't notice the physics changing.
  • A targeted diagnostic contribution, not a new architecture: gives the field a specific, measurable symptom to test future world models against, rather than the vague "compounding error" framing.
Show abstract
Long-horizon failure in world models is conventionally attributed to compounding error, a generic framing that does not distinguish what kind of error compounds. We propose a kinematic-vs-dynamic reframing: world models tend to imagine kinematically rather than dynamically. We operationalize this as the imagined Kinematic-Consistency Error, a per-step diagnostic that measures how far a rollout departs from a closed-form kinematic null, paired with a perturbation protocol that tests whether iKCE responds when physical conditions cross a regime boundary. We instantiate the diagnostic on a released DreamerV3 checkpoint trained on DMC walker-walk, where imagined iKCE runs roughly two orders of magnitude above that of matched real-physics rollouts. Across a friction sweep that crosses the gait-collapse boundary, the model's iKCE stays statistically flat even as the trained policy's reward collapses through the same range, providing the kinematic-not-dynamic signature. The diagnostic distinguishes kinematic from dynamic imagination at horizons longer than the embodiment's gait period.

EAGOR: Embodied Reasoning in Omni-direction

2026-07-07 cs.RO 👤 W. Yau (h=39)
Shriram Damodaran, Soumyaratna Debnath, Yan Wu, Wei-Yun Yau, Addison Lin Wang
Core Contributions
  • Reframes 360° directional reasoning as recursive Bayesian estimation directly on the sphere, rather than on the flattened equirectangular projection that most VLM-based omnidirectional systems inherit from perspective-image architectures.
  • Introduces a Spherical Harmonic Belief Field to propagate a continuous directional belief equivariantly under robot motion, eliminating ERP seam discontinuities and latitude distortion that break naive 2D-projection reasoning.
  • Training-free: works directly with existing VLM backbones without retraining, unlike task-specific navigation models built for one benchmark.
  • Quant: +34.4%/+45.6% relative gains on HOS/OSR-Bench, +14.6% navigation success, -17.7% steps, -24.5% angular error on a real legged robot — evidence the geometric fix, not more training, closes the gap.
Show abstract
Omni-directional (360°) cameras provide embodied agents with a holistic view of their surroundings, making them suited for directional reasoning in tasks such as navigation and object search. Existing Vision Language Models (VLMs) project 360° observations to 2D equirectangular projection (ERP) images and process them using architectures designed for perspective images. However, they ignore the spherical nature of 360° observations, where each pixel represents a viewing direction relative to the agent. Consequently, their direction estimates often become inconsistent under camera view transformations caused by agent motion. This limitation is particularly critical for map-free navigation, where the agent must continuously estimate the target direction in its egocentric frame. We propose EAGOR, a training-free, geometry-aware framework for embodied 360° directional reasoning. Instead of predicting target directions as ERP image coordinates, EAGOR formulates directional reasoning as recursive Bayesian estimation directly on the sphere. It maintains a continuous belief over target directions and propagates it equivariantly under agent motion without training the backbone VLMs. To achieve this, we introduce the Spherical Harmonic Belief Field (SH-BF), whose spherical harmonic representation provides a globally defined, rotation-aware basis for directional estimation on the spherical manifold. Robotic tree-fruit harvesting is a flagship problem for agricultural automation, but progress is bottlenecked by the cost and irreproducibility of field experiments: an orchard is available only weeks a year, every tree is different, and a control error can permanently damage the crop or the plant. The tree models used in graphics and agronomy are geometrically detailed but physically inert, while the GPU-parallel simulators used in robot learning contain no plausible trees. We present OrchardBench, a physically-grounded, GPU-parallel simulation of apple-orchard trees on the Newton engine.
