Research Landscape

Today's batch is dominated by Vision-Language-Action work — seven of 27 papers sit squarely in that cluster — but almost none of them propose a new end-to-end architecture from scratch. Instead the common move is to bolt something onto an existing or frozen VLA: Harness VLA wraps a completely frozen policy in an LLM-agent harness that hands off to analytic primitives outside its training distribution; TFP and Latent Memory Palace both inject a belief or reasoning state into an otherwise reactive policy, with TFP showing the belief update is 6x more active near contact/release events and causally (not just correlationally) shapes actions; and APIVOT interleaves visual "imagination" into an LLM planner to catch geometrically infeasible plans before execution. Meanwhile Temporal Ratio steps back and diagnoses why video-action models lose their pretrained compositional priors after finetuning, turning that diagnostic directly into an inference-time fix — a pattern of "understand the failure mode, then patch it without retraining" that shows up across several of today's VLA papers rather than the field simply scaling up parameters.

A second thread is the field's growing insistence that keypoint or pose tracking alone is not manipulation. ContactMimic makes this explicit by decoupling contact commands from keypoint trajectories (and shows via ablation that without careful data augmentation the policy just infers contact from geometry as a shortcut); AnyDexRT pushes calibration out of dexterous teleoperation entirely; and SkillPlug mines reusable, non-redundant skill primitives rather than training everything end-to-end per task. DexVerse ties this together as an evaluation problem: benchmarking Diffusion Policy, DP3, OpenVLA and π0.5 across 100 cross-embodiment tasks and finding all of them still show substantial generalization gaps — a useful counterweight to the strong single-benchmark numbers reported elsewhere in today's batch (e.g. FabriVLA's 90.0% on Meta-World, TFP's 98.75% on LIBERO).

A third theme is resource-constrained coordination: DeepCORD (learned distributed pose-graph solvers), D-CLIPSE (consensus localization via passive listening), and Early to Share, Late to Save (bandwidth-gated cooperative VLN) all wrestle with the same underlying question — how much do multiple robots really need to communicate — and land on a similar answer: share compact, well-timed information early rather than continuously. The VLN paper's counter-intuitive finding that agents communicate most when confident, not uncertain, is a notable departure from the usual entropy-gated intuition. Rounding out the batch, autonomous driving contributes a unifying post-training survey plus point solutions (anytime LiDAR detection, pedestrian face-swap privacy, intersection intention prediction), while control theory (cable-robot tension outside the feasible workspace, input-constrained safety tubes), simulation tooling (an Isaac Sim event-camera plugin), and human-robot interaction (a home-deployed dementia therapy robot, a lognormal-model comfort index) show a field still investing meaningfully outside the VLA mainstream.

Latent Memory Palace: Reasoning for Control as Autoregressive Variational Inference

2026-07-09 cs.LG, cs.RO Top author: K. Srinivasan (h=16)
Chuning Zhu, Eva Xu, Jose Barreiros, Krishnan Srinivasan, Paarth Shah
Core Contributions
  • ▸ Reframes 'reasoning' for continuous control as autoregressive variational inference over a latent space, sidestepping the granularity problem of reasoning directly in language for spatial/motor tasks.
  • ▸ Derives a tractable latent-space RL method to optimize the variational lower bound, rather than relying on LLM-style chain-of-thought that doesn't transfer well to motor precision.
  • ▸ Produces interpretable, adaptive test-time compute allocation — the policy visibly 'spends more steps' on harder decisions, echoing LLM reasoning behavior but grounded in action.
  • ▸ Spins off a variable-length action tokenizer (LMP-tok) as a byproduct, shown to boost downstream autoregressive policies independent of the main LMP-π controller.
Show abstract

Human decision-making is highly flexible -- some actions are taken immediately; others require longer deliberation. Language models have exhibited a similar capacity for adaptive "reasoning." However, transferring this capability to continuous control policies has been challenging, as directly reasoning in language space may lack the granularity for spatial understanding and precise motions. In this work, we show that reasoning for control policies can emerge by organizing information in an autoregressive latent space reminiscent of a memory palace, where retrieval is iterative and adaptive. Our method, Latent Memory Palace (LMP), formulates reasoning as variational inference with an autoregressive latent distribution. We derive a latent-space reinforcement learning technique to tractably optimize its variational lower bound. The resulting policy, LMP-$π$, achieves strong empirical performance in simulation and real-world domains while exhibiting interpretable, adaptive allocation of test-time compute. We further show that the same framework yields a variable-length action tokenizer, LMP-$\texttt{tok}$, which significantly improves the performance of downstream autoregressive policies. Together, these results present a new perspective on latent reasoning for control through the lens of variational inference.

Harness VLA: Steering Frozen VLAs into Reliable Manipulation Primitives via Memory-Guided Agents

2026-07-09 cs.RO Top author: Zhengru Fang (h=11)
Yixian Zhang, Huanming Zhang, Feng Gao, Xiao Li, Zhihao Liu
Core Contributions
  • ▸ Keeps a pretrained VLA completely frozen and wraps it with an LLM-coding-agent-style harness that composes it with a small fixed library of analytic primitives — avoiding the cost and risk of finetuning.
  • ▸ Learns the VLA's operating envelope from task-specific execution traces and failure models, so the harness knows when to retry the VLA versus hand off to an analytic primitive.
  • ▸ Reserves the frozen VLA specifically for local contact-rich phases while lifting semantic re-grounding and staging to the planner, splitting labor along a line prior single-model VLAs blur.
  • ▸ Reports concrete gains under deployment perturbations the base VLA wasn't trained for: +38.6 and +25.4 percentage points over the strongest baselines on LIBERO-Pro and RoboCasa365, and 58.4% success on RoboTwin C2R.
Show abstract

Language-conditioned manipulation requires both precise contact-rich control and robust reasoning over language, scenes, and long horizons. End-to-end Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models provide strong local visuomotor skills, but they are trained on in-distribution task trajectories and often fail under deployment perturbations such as semantic retargeting, goal re-binding, spatial-layout shifts, and unstable local contacts. LLM coding agents provide complementary semantic and compositional reasoning, but purely analytic primitives struggle with irregular grasping, constrained placement, and articulated-object interaction. We present Harness VLA, a memory-augmented agentic framework that exposes a frozen VLA as a retryable contact-rich primitive and composes it with a small fixed library of analytic primitives for grounding, staging, transport, navigation, and release. Rather than expanding the skill library, the harness learns the operating range of these fixed primitives from task-specific execution traces, global success rules, and failure models. By lifting semantic re-grounding, non-contact execution, and VLA re-staging to the planner while reserving the frozen VLA for local contact-rich phases, Harness VLA extends pretrained VLAs beyond their original trajectory distribution without finetuning. Across perturbed tabletop, household kitchen, and clean-to-randomized bimanual manipulation, Harness VLA improves over the strongest relevant baselines by 38.6 and 25.4 percentage points on LIBERO-Pro and RoboCasa365, respectively, and reaches 58.4% on RoboTwin C2R.

Native Video-Action Pretraining for Generalizable Robot Control

2026-07-09 cs.RO, cs.CV Top author: K. Cheng (h=10)
Qihang Zhang, Lin Li, Luyao Zhang, Shuai Yang, Yiming Luo
Core Contributions
  • ▸ Rejects repurposing video-generation VAEs (built for content creation) for robot control, training instead a semantic visual-action tokenizer from scratch that aligns representations with both semantics and actions.
  • ▸ Adopts causal pretraining rather than adapting bidirectional video architectures, explicitly to avoid the catastrophic forgetting the authors observed when bidirectional models are retrofitted for causal control.
  • ▸ Uses a sparse MoE backbone to expand model capacity for high-frequency inference without paying the full dense-model latency cost.
  • ▸ Introduces asynchronous inference that predicts future latents in parallel with action execution while re-grounding on the latest observation — directly addressing the latency problem that typically blocks video-action models from real-time closed-loop control.
Show abstract

The advent of video-action models offers a promising path for robot control. Nevertheless, we argue that repurposing video generative models designed for digital content creation is inherently inadequate for physical environments. To bridge this gap, we present LingBot-VA 2.0, a video-action foundation model built from the ground up for embodiment. Four core design principles showcase its evolution from LingBot-VA. (1) Departing from traditional reconstruction-focused VAEs, we introduce a semantic visual-action tokenizer, which aligns visual representations with both semantics and actions, improving instruction following and action precision in subsequent policy learning. (2) Given the strictly causal nature of temporal dynamics, we adopt a causal pretraining paradigm, training from scratch to circumvent the catastrophic forgetting that frequently occurs when adapting bidirectional architectures. (3) To meet the demands of high-frequency inference, our model employs a sparse MoE backbone, expanding model capacity without compromising efficiency. (4) Real-time closed-loop control is realized through an enhanced asynchronous inference scheme, which predicts future latents in parallel with action execution while re-grounding each rollout on the latest observation via learned forward dynamics. Real-world deployment validates LingBot-VA 2.0 as a robust foundation model, as evidenced by its few-shot generalization across complex manipulation tasks.

2026-07-09 cs.CV, cs.AI, cs.LG, cs.RO Top author: Joy Hsu (h=6)
Emily Jin, Joy Hsu, Yiqing Xu, Weiyu Liu, Nick Haber

  • ▸ Interleaves language and visual 'thoughts' adaptively within a single VLM-based planner, rather than committing to either pure language reasoning or pure visual simulation.
  • ▸ Uses visual thoughts specifically as imagined future states for internally verifying geometric feasibility — catching plans that are semantically fine but physically impossible, e.g. colliding or lacking free space.

Show abstract

Long-horizon robot planning requires jointly reasoning over semantic task structure and geometric feasibility. To successfully execute a task, a robot must decompose goals, select task-relevant objects, and sequence actions, while ensuring that plans satisfy spatial constraints such as limited free space and object collisions. In this work, we propose APIVOT, a VLM-based planner that adaptively interleaves language and visual thoughts for long-horizon planning. APIVOT learns to leverage language for semantic reasoning, while using visual thoughts as imagined future states for internal verification of geometric feasibility. On long-horizon kitchen tasks, APIVOT outperforms general-purpose VLMs and prior planning frameworks, achieving the largest gains in spatially constrained settings. We find that APIVOT learns meaningful modality selection behavior, demonstrating that adaptive interleaving of vision-language thoughts improves both planning success and reasoning efficiency.

We present FabriVLA, a lightweight Vision-Language-Action model for Precise Multi-Task Manipulation. Understanding and Mitigating the Video-Action Generalization Gap via Temporal Ratio

2026-07-09 cs.CV, cs.RO Top author: U. Mishra (h=4)
Utkarsh A. Mishra, Yongxin Chen, Danfei Xu, Yang Liu, Xi Chen
Core Contributions
  • ▸ Names and formalizes the 'video-action generalization gap': world/video-action models lose the compositional priors of their video pretraining after finetuning on robot action data.
  • ▸ Surveys a broad design space of video-action models and finds no single architectural choice reliably explains which models keep compositional generalization — motivating a new diagnostic rather than another architecture tweak.
  • ▸ Introduces Temporal Ratio (TR), an attention-based measure of how much the action head leans on future latent rollouts versus the current frame, and shows TR predicts a model's compositional generalization capacity.
  • ▸ Turns the diagnostic into an inference-time fix — an adaptive guidance method that amplifies compositional video conditioning exactly when TR indicates reliance on future rollouts — mitigating the OOD-ID gap on LIBERO and real-world tasks without retraining.
Show abstract

Generative video foundation models exhibit strong compositional priors, yet world-action models (WAMs) and video-action models (VAMs) often lose these priors after finetuning on robotic action data. We refer to this discrepancy as the video-action generalization gap. In this paper, we systematically investigate this gap by evaluating a comprehensive design space of VAMs, demonstrating that standard design choices yield no emergent explanation pattern. To explain this behavior, we introduce the Temporal Ratio (TR), an attention-based measure of how strongly the action head relies on future latent rollouts relative to the anchored current frame. TR has two key properties: first, a model's structural reliance on future-predictive latents, measured via TR, acts as a predictor of its compositional generalization capacity; second, it natively fluctuates based on task phase, shifting attention to future frames during planning and reverting to the present frame for precise manipulation. Finally, based on these findings, we propose an inference-time adaptive guidance method, which exploits this intrinsic feature attention pattern to dynamically amplify compositional video conditioning signals precisely when the policy relies on future rollouts. Evaluated on the LIBERO benchmark and real-world tasks, our approach mitigates the OOD-ID compositional generalization gap. More details: https://umishra.me/temporal-ratio/

TFP: Temporally Conditioned Memory-Fusion Policies for Visuomotor Learning

2026-07-09 cs.RO Top author: Yushen Liang (h=1)
Yushen Liang, Yue Peng, Baosheng Jin, Tianluo Zhang, Xinyu Zhang
Core Contributions
  • ▸ Argues current VLA policies like π0.5 and OpenVLA are reactive — mapping current observation to next action — which breaks in stage-dependent tasks where the same visual state should trigger different actions depending on task progress.
  • ▸ Introduces an episode-local task-progress belief updated with Liquid Time-Constant dynamics, injected directly into the flow-matching action decoder via adaptive modulation rather than treated as passive history context.
  • ▸ Shows the belief update is selectively active: write-gain changes near contact/release/subgoal events are about 6x larger than during stable phases, and hidden-state interventions demonstrate the belief causally shapes generated actions rather than merely correlating with them.
  • ▸ Improves a 3.3B-parameter model from 96.9% to 98.75% on LIBERO and 91.4% to 93.77% on LIBERO-plus, with the memory-focused MIKASA ShellGameTouch diagnostic reaching 75.0% — targeted precisely at the occlusion/stage-transition cases the method was designed for.
Show abstract

Vision--Language--Action (VLA) policies such as $π_{0.5}$ and OpenVLA perform well on many manipulation tasks, but they are often reactive: the next action is predicted from the current observation, instruction, and proprioceptive state. This assumption breaks down in stage-dependent manipulation, where visually similar states may require different actions depending on latent task progress and previous interaction outcomes. We argue that such tasks require not only memory, but dynamics-aware belief updates: the policy should preserve task progress during stable or occluded phases and revise its belief near contact, release, or subgoal transitions. We introduce Temporally Conditioned Memory-Fusion Policies (TFP), a lightweight memory-action framework for VLA backbones. TFP maintains an episode-local task-progress belief with Liquid Time-Constant dynamics and injects the updated belief directly into the flow-matching action decoder through adaptive modulation. This lets temporally accumulated context shape the generated action chunk, rather than serving only as passive history context. With a 3.3B-parameter model, TFP improves the average success rate from \(96.9\%\) to \(98.75\%\) on LIBERO and from \(91.4\%\) to \(93.77\%\) on LIBERO-plus. On the memory-focused MIKASA ShellGameTouch diagnostic, TFP achieves success up to \(75.0\%\). Mechanistic analyses show that write-gain changes near manipulation events are about \(6\times\) larger than far non-event phases, and hidden-state interventions show that the belief causally modulates generated action chunks. These results suggest that compact, event-sensitive memory dynamics can improve VLA policies under occlusion, visual perturbation, and stage-dependent task structure.

ContactMimic: Humanoid Object Interaction via Contact Control

2026-07-09 cs.RO Top author: Runpei Dong (h=17)
Xinyao Li, Xialin He, Runpei Dong, Saurabh Gupta
Core Contributions
  • ▸ Argues keypoint-tracking imitation is fundamentally blind to contact: a robot can reach the target pose without ever meaningfully touching the object, breaking tasks like wiping or sitting.
  • ▸ Introduces explicit part-level binary contact commands as a second control channel alongside keypoint trajectories, giving the policy independent control over whether contact occurs.
  • ▸ Uses a trajectory augmentation scheme designed specifically to decorrelate keypoint motion from contact labels — ablations show without it the policy shortcuts by inferring contact from geometry alone.
  • ▸ Achieves contact-controllability (produce or suppress contact on command at deployment), validated sim-to-real across 5 real-world motions after training on 10 simulated human-object interactions.
Show abstract

Keypoint tracking alone is insufficient for object interaction tasks such as sitting on a chair, wiping a board, or pushing furniture, where the robot can reach the correct pose without making meaningful physical contact with the object. We present CONTACTMIMIC, a learning framework that tracks explicit partlevel binary contact commands alongside keypoint trajectories. CONTACTMIMIC is made possible through the use of contact-following rewards and a trajectory augmentation scheme aimed at breaking the correlations between keypoint trajectories and contact labels. The resulting policy successfully decouples contact behavior from keypoint geometry, and achieves precise physical contact as well as contact-controllability (produce or suppress contact during deployment as desired). Simulation experiments across 10 diverse human-object interaction motions confirm that CONTACTMIMIC exhibits contact controllability that enables it to complete manipulation tasks without task-specific rewards, while also outperforming keypoint-only trackers on contact-relevant tasks. Ablations confirm the necessity of the proposed trajectory augmentation scheme and sim2real deployment validates contact controllability in the real world across 5 different motions. Video results are available on https://lixinyao11.github.io/contactmimic-page/.

Core Contributions
  • ▸ Removes the calibration step most retargeting pipelines require, replacing hand-crafted objectives and global shape-matching with self-supervised fingertip correspondence learning.
  • ▸ Anchors the human-to-robot mapping using only a few-shot human demonstration rather than dense calibration data, cutting setup cost for each new dexterous hand.
  • ▸ Adds a dedicated contact classifier to refine pinch-related poses, addressing a fine-grained failure mode that generic correspondence learning tends to miss.

Teleoperation is a key interface for controlling dexterous robotic hands and collecting demonstrations for imitation learning. Its effectiveness largely depends on kinematic retargeting, which maps operator hand motions to feasible and intuitive robot hand motions. Existing methods often require hand-crafted objectives, precise calibration, or global shape matching between human and robot hand spaces, making them sensitive to hand-specific tuning and less reliable across different dexterous hands. We propose AnyDexRT, a calibration-free retargeting method for intuitive dexterous teleoperation across human-like dexterous hands. SkillPlug: Unsupervised Skill Mining for Few-Shot Adaptation in Robotic Manipulation

2026-07-09 cs.RO Top author: Ziwei Wang (h=8)
Zi-han Ding, Ziwei Wang
Core Contributions
  • ▸ Adds a skill-conditioning module on top of an existing visuomotor policy rather than replacing it, mining a reusable skill library from raw multi-task demonstrations without extra supervision.
  • ▸ Uses self-supervised objectives that specifically push skills toward being compact, reusable, and non-redundant — targeting the composability failure mode of end-to-end policies.
  • ▸ After mining, freezes the skill library and adapts to new tasks by fine-tuning only a lightweight router and action head, avoiding full end-to-end retraining per task.
  • ▸ Validated on two simulation benchmarks and a real robot, with mined skills improving both multi-task performance and few-shot adaptation — evidence the skills transfer rather than overfitting to one benchmark.
Show abstract

Learning transferable visuomotor imitation policies that generalize across diverse manipulation tasks and adapt rapidly to new tasks from only a handful of demonstrations remains challenging. Most modern policies are trained end-to-end to map observations directly to low-level actions, offering little explicit structure for reusing and recombining behaviors across tasks and making transfer data-inefficient under limited supervision. We propose SkillPlug, a plug-in framework that augments an existing visuomotor policy with a skill-conditioning module and mines a shared, transferable skill library from raw multi-task demonstrations. SkillPlug learns skills via self-supervised objectives that promote compact, reusable, and non-redundant behavior-level primitives, forming a task-shared prior for compositional control. After skill mining, we keep the learned skills fixed and specialize to unseen tasks by fine-tuning only lightweight router and action head, enabling efficient adaptation without full end-to-end retraining. We evaluate SkillPlug on two simulation benchmarks and on a real robot, and observe that the mined transferable skills consistently improve both multi-task performance and few-shot adaptation. Overall, SkillPlug offers a scalable way to mine reusable skills that improve data-efficient generalization in robotic manipulation.
