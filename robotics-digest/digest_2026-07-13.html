Research Landscape

Today's 30-paper batch is anchored by a cluster of papers all wrestling with how actions and scenes should be represented for embodied foundation models, and a recurring specific sub-problem is coordinate-frame mismatch. Mixture of Frames Policy shows that the best action frame for a diffusion policy is task-dependent and builds a mixture-of-frames denoiser that beats oracle single-frame selection; See like a Robot attacks a related but distinct mismatch — VLAs observe in the camera frame but act in the robot frame — by injecting robot-centric pointmaps into existing pi0.5 and SmolVLA backbones with only "minimal architectural change." Both papers converge on the same insight from opposite directions: geometry, not just semantics, is a first-class citizen that pretrained 2D-image VLAs currently handle poorly by default. Sitting alongside them, EDAR and WALA both push back on treating actions as pure control-signal sequences — EDAR grounds action tokens in their environment-conditioned visual effects, while WALA mines executable latent actions from action-free video by predicting DINOv3 feature and depth deltas rather than pixels, reaching a new RoboCasa state of the art (75.2%) in the process.

A second thread is squeezing more capability out of scarce labeled robot data rather than scaling it. REGRIND learns contact-rich dexterous manipulation (scissors, screwdrivers) from a single human demonstration via contact-preserving retargeting plus residual RL; TeleDexter reaches 75% success on in-hand dexterity tasks where "all baselines consistently fail" using only single-stage RL on human-motion subgoals; and Xiaomi-Robotics-U0 takes the opposite tack of using a 38B-parameter generative world model as a synthetic data engine, lifting pi_0.5's out-of-distribution success from 36.9% to 63.2%. The 30,000-foot view offered by From World Action Models to Embodied Brains ties this together, arguing the field's real bottleneck isn't model capability but fragmented action spaces, datasets, and runtime interfaces across exactly these efforts — a diagnosis this batch's diversity of representations seems to confirm.

A third, quieter theme is a return to physical grounding and certified correctness, in contrast to the learning-heavy cluster above. Robust bipedal locomotion on flowable slopes and NeuralActuator both dig into low-level physical interaction — cleat-terrain mechanics and actuator hysteresis/backlash, respectively — that no amount of policy learning papers over. On the control-theory side, three separate papers pursue formal certification rather than empirical benchmarks alone: the Active Noise Floor / VoNI paper certifies when reliability covers hold under unknown sensing noise, the UAV Human Intent paper formalizes maneuver preconditions/postconditions for runtime verification, and the QE/CGS trajectory planning paper uses symbolic Gröbner-system computation to certify IK solutions exist along an entire path. None of these three cite each other, but together they suggest a small but persistent counter-current to the field's dominant empirical-benchmark culture. Rounding out the batch, hardware papers (MIRA's open UAV platform, the endovascular intervention robot, automated animatronic facial mechanism synthesis) and HRI work (the 15.3% nonverbal-cue-triggered interaction finding, ERR@HRI 3.0's naturalistic error-detection challenge) show the field still investing broadly outside the VLA/foundation-model mainstream.

From World Action Models to Embodied Brains: A Roadmap for Open-World Physical Intelligence

2026-07-13 cs.RO, cs.AI
Yuanzhi Liang, Xufeng Zhan, Haibin Huang, Chi Zhang, Xuelong Li
Core Contributions
  • ▸ Surveys progress toward World Action Models (WAMs) and argues the field's fragmentation — incompatible action spaces, inconsistent dataset/task conventions, and limited runtime interfaces — is now the main blocker, not raw model capability.
  • ▸ Proposes the 'embodied brain' as a long-term target: a model that issues state-transition or capability requests rather than direct actuator commands, explicitly separating high-level intent from low-level execution.
  • ▸ Frames WAMs as predictive-function prototypes within this larger stack, paired with a physical harness (tools, controllers, verification, trace logging) that grounds model outputs in the real world.
  • ▸ Proposes shared contracts across heterogeneous models, data, tasks, and embodiments plus closed-loop post-training that converts verified interaction into reusable experience — a concrete mechanism for the self-improvement loop the roadmap argues the field needs.
Show abstract

Artificial general intelligence ultimately requires agents that can reason and act in the physical world. Action models, vision-language-action policies, and world models have advanced this goal, while World Action Models (WAMs) are particularly promising because they connect candidate interventions with predicted consequences. However, progress remains fragmented: models use incompatible action spaces and prediction targets, datasets and tasks follow different conventions, and runtime systems expose limited interfaces for reuse and evaluation. We review the evolution toward WAMs and organize these limitations into three coupled gaps: model roles and representations, objectives and standardization, and system composition. Building on this analysis, we propose a co-evolution roadmap for physical intelligence centered on the \emph{embodied brain}, a long-term model target for integrating multimodal context, comparing candidate interventions, and issuing state-transition or capability requests rather than direct actuator commands. WAMs provide promising prototypes for its predictive functions, while a physical harness grounds model outputs through tools, controllers, verification, and trace logging. Shared contracts align heterogeneous models, data, tasks, and embodiments, and closed-loop post-training converts verified interaction into reusable experience. Together, these components define a modular physical-intelligence stack for adaptive and self-improving embodied agents.

See like a Robot: Robot-Centric Pointmaps for Vision-Language-Action Models

2026-07-13 cs.RO, cs.AI
Byungkun Lee, Dongyoon Hwang, Dongjin Kim, Hojoon Lee, Minho Park
Core Contributions
  • ▸ Identifies a specific architectural mismatch in VLAs: actions are defined in the robot's 3D frame but observations arrive in the camera frame — invisible under a fixed camera setup, but harmful once training data spans multiple camera placements.
  • ▸ Fixes this with robot-centric pointmaps — images whose pixels encode 3D scene coordinates in the robot frame — chosen because they preserve the dense H×W grid pretrained 2D VLAs already expect, requiring minimal architectural change to adopt.
  • ▸ Plugs into two different existing VLAs (π0.5 and SmolVLA) and improves both on RoboCasa, beating representative camera-viewpoint and 3D-aware baselines, showing the fix generalizes across backbones.
  • ▸ On real-robot tests, the advantage over an RGB-only policy widens specifically when the camera is moved to a placement unseen during training — direct evidence the gain traces to fixing the frame mismatch, not added capacity.
Show abstract

Vision-language-action (VLA) models predict robot actions from visual observations and language instructions. These actions are defined in the robot's own 3D coordinate frame, yet most VLAs observe the scene in the camera frame, creating a frame mismatch between where the scene is observed and where actions are defined. The mismatch is benign under a fixed viewpoint, where the policy can memorize a single observation-to-action mapping, but grows harder as large-scale datasets aggregate demonstrations across diverse camera setups and the policy must generalize this mapping across viewpoints. We address this mismatch with robot-centric pointmaps, images whose pixels store the 3D coordinates of scene points in the robot frame. Pointmaps provide robot-frame 3D geometry while preserving the dense H x W grid expected by pretrained 2D VLAs, so they integrate into existing VLAs with minimal architectural change. On RoboCasa, pointmaps improve both pi0.5 and SmolVLA and outperform representative camera-viewpoint and 3D-aware baselines. In real-robot experiments, their advantage over an RGB-only policy widens when the camera is moved to a placement unseen during training.

EDAR: Learning Environment-Dependent Action Representations for Robotic Manipulation

2026-07-13 cs.RO
Yuecheng Xu, Tong Yang, Jingkai Jia, Chi Zhang, Xuelong Li
Core Contributions
  • ▸ Argues existing action-representation methods encode only the structure of the action stream itself, treating the environment's role as implicit, when the same action segment can produce different outcomes depending on scene context.
  • ▸ Grounds action tokens in both executable control structure and their expected visual consequences, coupling motor commands with environment-conditioned effects rather than command patterns alone.
  • ▸ Aims explicitly to capture interaction semantics — what an action does to the world — rather than just command-level regularities that the paper argues existing representations miss.
  • ▸ Shows gains concentrated in long-horizon manipulation specifically, consistent with environment-grounding mattering more once errors can compound across many action-effect steps.
Show abstract

Learning effective action representations is critical for robotic manipulation, where raw control trajectories are often noisy, redundant, and difficult to model directly. Existing methods mainly encode the structure of the action stream itself, treating the role of actions in the environment as implicit. Yet manipulation is about changing the world: the same action segment can induce different outcomes under different scene contexts, making action semantics inherently environment-dependent. We propose EDAR, an Environment-Dependent Action Representation that grounds action tokens in both executable control structure and expected visual consequences. By coupling motor commands with their environment-conditioned effects, EDAR encourages the learned action space to capture interaction semantics rather than merely command-level patterns. Experiments on simulated and real-robot manipulation benchmarks demonstrate that EDAR improves downstream policy learning, especially in long-horizon manipulation. These results highlight the importance of grounding action representations in executable control structure and environment-conditioned visual change.

WALA Learning Executable Latent Actions from Action-Labeled Demonstrations and Action-Free Videos

2026-07-13 cs.RO
Jiahao Liu, Zhongpu Xia, Shuai Tian, Huangrui Li, Yuhang Zheng
Core Contributions
  • ▸ Learns executable latent actions from both action-labeled robot demos and action-free human/robot video, addressing the fact that labeled demos are expensive while unlabeled video is abundant but lacks action supervision.
  • ▸ Pretrains the latent action model to predict future deltas in DINOv3 feature space and dense depth space rather than reconstructing raw pixels, preserving task-relevant semantic/geometric structure while ignoring appearance noise.
  • ▸ During policy training, jointly supervises latent actions with robot action prediction, latent action target matching, and future dynamics prediction, letting action-free video contribute dynamics supervision even without action labels.
  • ▸ Sets a new state-of-the-art on RoboCasa with 75.2% average success and also improves RoboTwin and real-world generalization, indicating the video-derived dynamics signal transfers into concrete task performance.
Show abstract

Generalizable robot policies typically rely on action-labeled robot demonstrations, which are expensive to collect and difficult to scale. In contrast, large-scale human and robot videos contain rich physical interactions but often lack executable robot action labels. We present WALA, a framework for learning executable latent actions from both action-labeled demonstrations and action-free videos. WALA first pretrains a semantic-geometric latent action model from videos by modeling the evolution between current observations and sparsely sampled future observations. Instead of reconstructing raw pixels, WALA predicts future deltas in the DINOv3 feature space and dense depth space, preserving task-relevant semantic and geometric structure while reducing sensitivity to appearance details. During policy training, the pretrained encoder provides stable latent action targets, and the decoder serves as a trainable latent world model. The latent actions generated by the vision-language backbone are jointly supervised by robot action prediction, latent action target matching, and future dynamics prediction. This enables action-labeled demonstrations to provide executable control supervision, while action-free videos contribute dynamics supervision without requiring robot action annotations. Experiments show that WALA achieves strong performance on RoboTwin, sets a new state-of-the-art result on RoboCasa with 75.2% average success, and improves both policy performance and generalization in real-world manipulation tasks.

Mixture of Frames Policy: Multi-Frame Action Denoising for Bimanual Mobile Manipulation

2026-07-13 cs.RO
Dian Wang, Jisang Park, Xiaomeng Xu, Han Zhang, Shuran Song
Core Contributions
  • ▸ Diffusion policies typically commit to one fixed action coordinate frame; MoF instead runs synchronized denoising across several frames at once from a single canonical diffusion state re-expressed per frame.
  • ▸ Introduces a column-based 6D rotation parameterization that supports exact, differentiable frame transforms even on noisy intermediate diffusion states, avoiding the usual problem of noisy rotations falling off the SO(3) manifold.
  • ▸ Shows the best action frame is task-dependent, and MoF beats even oracle single-frame selection and standard Mixture-of-Experts baselines across nine simulated bimanual tasks — the frame-fusion mechanism itself, not just picking the right frame, drives the gain.
  • ▸ Validated on two real bimanual mobile manipulation tasks, outperforming every constituent single-frame baseline, evidence the benefit survives the sim-to-real gap.
Show abstract

Robotic manipulation is inherently multi-frame: local actions may be simple in an end-effector frame, while transport, upright-object handling, and whole-body coordination are better represented in a base-aligned frame. However, modern diffusion-based visuomotor policies typically commit to a single predefined action frame, forcing one denoiser to model action distributions that are often unnecessarily complex in that frame. We propose Mixture of Frames Policy (MoF), a diffusion policy that performs synchronized action denoising across multiple coordinate frames. MoF maintains a single canonical diffusion state, re-expresses it in several task-relevant frames, applies frame-specialized denoisers, and fuses their noise predictions back in the canonical frame. To make this possible for intermediate noisy diffusion states, we introduce a column-based 6D rotation representation within an SE(3) action parameterization that supports exact, differentiable frame transformations without requiring noisy rotations to lie on the SO(3) manifold. Across nine simulated bimanual manipulation tasks, we show that the best action frame is task-dependent and that MoF improves over oracle frame selection and standard Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) baselines. We further evaluate MoF on two real-world bimanual mobile manipulation tasks, demonstrating that it outperforms all constituent single-frame baselines. Project homepage: https://mofpo.github.io

Yunhai Feng, Natalie Leung, Jiaxuan Wang, Lujie Yang, Haozhi Qi
Core Contributions
  • ▸ Learns dexterous manipulation from a single human demonstration by retargeting to a robot reference that explicitly preserves hand-object contact relationships, not just kinematic pose.
  • ▸ Trains a residual RL policy in simulation to track object-centric keypoints along that retargeted reference rather than imitating the demo directly, recovering physically consistent contact where retargeting alone is imperfect.

Show abstract

Recent work in humanoid whole-body control has found success with a simple recipe: retarget human motion to robot kinematic references, then train policies via reinforcement learning (RL) to track them. But how does this recipe transfer to dexterous manipulation? The answer is not obvious, as manipulation involves complex, contact-rich dynamics and requires delicate regulation of contact modes and forces. We present REGRIND, a minimalist retargeting-guided RL pipeline that learns dexterous manipulation policies from a single human demonstration. REGRIND retargets human hand-object motion to a robot reference that preserves hand-object spatial and contact relationships, trains a residual RL policy in simulation to track object-centric keypoints along that reference, and transfers the resulting policy zero-shot to hardware with careful system identification. The resulting policies produce fluid, human-like behavior on two different multi-fingered hands across contact-rich tool-use tasks, including operating a pair of scissors and turning a screwdriver. Through systematic hardware experiments, we identify and analyze the key factors that govern sim-to-real transfer in dexterous manipulation, offering practical guidance for retargeting-based learning in contact-rich settings. Towards Human-level Dexterous Teleoperation

2026-07-13 cs.RO
Puhao Li, Zeyuan Chen, Yingying Wu, Pengkun Wei, Yuyang Li
Core Contributions
  • ▸ Targets in-hand dexterity — tool regrasping, reorientation, finger gaiting within a single hand — that current teleoperation systems handle poorly despite humans doing it seamlessly.
  • ▸ Trains a hand-object co-tracking controller on subgoals derived from human reference motion using a hybrid reward that couples sparse subgoal objectives with dense tracking, letting the policy learn across varied interaction modes instead of imitating exact frame-by-frame trajectories.
  • ▸ Needs only single-stage RL, and with random action masking plus domain randomization, transfers zero-shot to real hardware with no separate sim-to-real fine-tuning stage.

Show abstract

Humans routinely wield tools, swap grasps, and reposition objects within a single hand, seamlessly orchestrating contact transitions that span translation, reorientation, and finger gaiting. Endowing robot dexterous hands with this level of in-hand dexterity through teleoperation requires precise control of object motion via dynamic hand-object contact, yet current teleoperation systems remain far from this capability. To bridge this gap, we take a major step towards human-level dexterous teleoperation by introducing TeleDexter, a hand-object co-tracking controller that maps operator intent into learned, low-level contact execution. Furthermore, the collected demonstrations successfully train autonomous policies via behavioral cloning, marking a concrete step towards human-level dexterous teleoperation.

Robust bipedal locomotion on flowable slopes via foot-driven terrain manipulation

2026-07-13 cs.RO
Deniz Kerimoglu, Junnosuke Kamohara, Jiyeon Maeng, Ziwon Yoon, Seth Hutchinson
Core Contributions
  • ▸ Treats terrain response as something the robot can actively control through foot design, a limb-centric alternative to the usual body-centric compensation for terrain effects.
  • ▸ Systematically varies cleat spacing on a robophysical biped and finds sparse spacing over-yields the substrate while dense spacing over-resists it; only an intermediate spacing keeps substrate stress near the yield threshold.
  • ▸ The tuned intermediate-spacing foot enables walking on granular slopes up to 30 degrees — a concrete quantitative ceiling for what foot design alone buys without changing gait or body control.
  • ▸ Adds an actively depth-adjusting cleat foot that handles both rigid and granular ground, then shows the principle scales from a 1.4 kg robophysical model to a 15 kg autonomous biped.
Show abstract

Bipedal robots are challenging to control because they operate close to instability, where small variations in foot-terrain contact can rapidly destabilize locomotion. On rigid terrain, bipedal robots mitigate this fragility by using well-established contact mechanics and control strategies. On flowable surfaces such as granular slopes, foot contact can induce large surface deformations and solid-fluid-like transitions, coupling terrain effects with robot dynamics, leading to underperformance or failure. This is partly due to the lack of reliable methods to represent the dynamics of flowable terrain, making it difficult to account for terrain effects in locomotion design. Here, we investigate how controlling terrain response can improve bipedal locomotion on granular slopes by studying the terradynamics of cleated feet, thin plates emanating from the foot soles. Systematic studies of a small-scale (1.4 kg) robophysical biped reveal that cleats with sparse and dense spacing lead to excessive terrain yielding and resistance, respectively, degrading performance and leading to failure. An intermediate cleat spacing distributes interaction forces to maintain substrate stresses near (or below) the yield threshold, enabling walking on granular slopes up to 30 degrees. Guided by these principles, we design a foot that actively adjusts cleat depth and accommodates both rigid and granular terrain. We also demonstrate that the principles of effective foot-terrain interaction translate to a larger (15 kg) autonomous biped. Our study presents an alternative to conventional body-centric robot control approaches, which regulate terrain-induced effects through body motion, by instead regulating terrain interactions through limb-centric approach.

2026-07-13 cs.RO, cs.LG
Evelyn D'Elia, Weishu Zhan, Giulio Turrisi, Giulio Romualdi, Giuseppe L'Erario
Core Contributions
  • ▸ Combines two previously separate efficiency tricks for legged-robot RL — morphological symmetry and Koopman-operator dynamics models — training a Koopman model of system dynamics jointly with the policy rather than using either technique alone.
  • ▸ Feeds the learned Koopman predictions to the critic as privileged observations, giving it smoother, more informative features than raw proprioception without changing what the actor can access at deployment.
  • ▸ Bakes group symmetries into actor, critic, encoder, and decoder simultaneously, producing a policy that is highly equivariant rather than only approximately symmetric through data augmentation.

Show abstract

Reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms classically suffer from poor sample efficiency. In robotics, a recent line of work has emerged addressing this problem by encoding physics priors in the learning process. However, most of these approaches are validated on well-defined, low-dimensional benchmark systems rather than high-dimensional robots with complex nonlinear dynamics. In this paper, we introduce \textit{SKooP (Symmetric Koopman Predictions)}, an approach combining the advantages of morphological symmetries with those of a Koopman model learned via autoencoder to enhance policy learning. Active Noise Floor Estimation for Reliability-Optimal POMDPs: A Value-of-Noise-Information Approach

2026-07-13 eess.SY, cs.RO
Hyung-Jin Yoon
Core Contributions
  • ▸ Extends Finite Reliability Representations (FRR), which certify when a cell-constant policy suffices, to the realistic case where the physical noise floor itself is unknown and possibly context-dependent.
  • ▸ Defines Value of Noise Information (VoNI) as the certificate-gap cost of acting on a stale noise estimate instead of the true parameter, giving a principled trigger for when active probing is worth it.
  • ▸ Shows analytically that noise estimation has low decision value away from the FRR crossover regime but becomes critical once posterior uncertainty threatens to invalidate the current reliability cover.
  • ▸ In closed-loop UGV simulations, the certificate-aware trigger detects abrupt sensor-noise jumps earlier and needs substantially fewer probing actions than posterior-entropy exploration across 50 Monte Carlo trials.
Show abstract

Finite Reliability Representations (FRR) certify when a cell-constant policy is sufficient for reliable decision-making in a partially observed system with a known physical noise floor. In practice, however, sensing and execution noise can be latent and context-dependent. This paper develops a certificate-aware active disambiguation framework for an unknown physical noise parameter theta = (sigma_y, sigma_u), with the sensor-only case obtained by fixing sigma_u. We define the Value of Noise Information (VoNI) as the expected excess FRR certificate gap caused by using a reliability cover calibrated to the current estimate rather than to the realized noise parameter. We bound VoNI using action-value model mismatch and FRR radius inflation, showing that noise estimation has low decision value in sub-crossover regimes where the FRR certificate is insensitive to theta, but becomes valuable when posterior uncertainty can invalidate the current cover. A bi-level decision maker uses a posterior over theta, obtained from innovation statistics, execution residuals, or another online estimator, and triggers diagnostic probing only when uncertainty threatens the FRR certificate. We also interpret VoNI as a tractable, certificate-aware approximation to a high-level finite POMDP for latent sensing-execution regime disambiguation. Under stationary, identifiable, and persistently exciting regimes, we establish posterior consistency and convergence of the induced policy loss to the FRR approximation floor. Closed-loop UGV simulations with EKF-based innovation residuals show earlier detection of abrupt sensing-noise jumps, lower drift-tracking error, and substantially fewer probing actions than posterior-entropy exploration over 50 Monte Carlo trials.
