Research Landscape

Today's 30 papers show world-action and VLA models leaning increasingly on explicit geometric verification rather than trusting raw learned rollouts. Test-Time Scaling for World Action Models judges whether extra inference compute is worth spending by checking cross-view depth-reprojection consistency of predicted futures against a frozen geometry model, while GeoWorldAD grounds autonomous-driving trajectory planning in explicit present and latent future 3D geometry rather than relying on vision/video-conditioned action generation alone. Both treat geometric consistency as the missing ingredient scale hasn't supplied. A parallel efficiency thread runs through Patch Policy, which shows dense ViT patch tokens under a block-causal attention mask beat fine-tuned OpenVLA-OFT by 18% while using roughly 0.7% of its parameters — arguing architecture, not backbone size, was the bottleneck — even as RynnBrain 1.1 scales the opposite direction (2B to 122B) but pairs that scale with targeted additions like contact-point prediction and native 3D grounding rather than raw parameter growth alone. RoboHarness closes the loop on this diversity of policy types by orchestrating VLAs, RL policies, and TAMP systems as interchangeable "agentic skills" rather than assuming one architecture wins.

A second cluster tackles manipulation's core information problem: single-frame or short-history observations can't disambiguate contact events, occlusion, or long-horizon object state. FM-VLA replaces expensive vision-based memory with compact force-history tokens specifically because repeated small-motion contacts — like pressing a button multiple times — are visually ambiguous but mechanically obvious, while Closing the Loop in Humanoid VLA names this failure "object-state divergence" and nearly doubles a matched GR00T-N1.7 baseline (39/80 → 71/80) by using the same persistent 3D object record for both acting and verifying success. HCPG-Flow, Manifold-Guided Motion Planning, and Leveraging Two Robotic Arms all push the same instinct further into contact geometry itself — biasing sampling toward the "critical manifold" of near-zero-clearance assembly configurations, or scoring flow-policy proposals by post-contact task progress — rather than leaving contact structure implicit in a learned policy. ConceptTree extends this preference for structured, inspectable intermediate representations from perception into decision-making, replacing opaque skill-selection latents with a decision tree over human-interpretable concepts.

A third theme is a marked preference for validating claims against real hardware and messier operating conditions rather than clean simulation. Does Robust VIO Need More Learning? finds that confining learning to correspondence proposals while keeping geometric verification explicit outperforms deeper end-to-end learned VIO precisely under real distribution shift — motion blur, illumination change, indoor-outdoor transitions — a direct counterpoint to "learn more of the pipeline." Importance Sampling and PCA for Finding Failures scales rare-event discovery methods, previously validated mostly on academic driving stacks, to an actual commercial autonomous trucking planner. Value-Aware MARO and RT-SHCUA both treat degraded operating conditions — communication loss, real-time safety-critical UAV control — as first-class design constraints rather than edge cases layered on afterward. The same real-world bias shows up in hardware: MEVION and the Duke Robotics AUV design review both report concrete, low-cost, physically deployed systems (a $14,000 dual-arm data-collection rig, a competition AUV with a redesigned acoustics PCB), and Isaac Sim-to-Real, the NICO grasping and gesture papers, and Seg2Grasp all report physical-robot results rather than simulation-only success rates — a batch broadly skeptical of results untested outside simulation.

Test-Time Scaling for World Action Models via Zero-Shot Geometric Evaluation
2026-07-20 cs.RO Top author: Yulong Cao (h=17)
Zesen Zhao, Minkyoung Cho, Hui shen, Boyuan Zheng, Kunxiao Gao
Core Contributions
  • Targets a decision robot control faces that generic LLM test-time scaling doesn't: whether extra inference compute is worth spending before an action executes, since a robot can't revise a physical action after the fact.
  • Introduces a training-free Best-of-N selector that scores World Action Model rollouts by cross-view depth-reprojection consistency of their predicted futures using a frozen geometry foundation model, rather than a learned or task-specific critic — the same check generalizes zero-shot across backbones.
  • Adds a lightweight consistency gate that only triggers extra sampling when the initial rollout looks internally inconsistent, recovering 74.8% of the always-on accuracy gain while resampling on only 26.2% of decision points.
  • Across five benchmark-backbone settings on RoboCasa, LIBERO Long, and RoboTwin 2.0, raises N=8 success (e.g. RoboCasa average 66.3%→68.4% with Cosmos Policy, 80.8%→82.5% with X-WAM).
  • Identifies false low-score selections as a specific failure mode explaining why naive Best-of-N scaling can saturate or degrade as N grows — a caution against assuming more samples always helps.
Show abstract

Test-time scaling improves foundation-model inference by spending additional computation, but robot control requires deciding whether extra compute is useful before executing an action. World Action Models (WAMs) make this decision natural: each rollout exposes both an action chunk and predicted future observations. We propose \methodgated, a training-free selective test-time scaling framework for WAMs. We first instantiate \method, a fixed-budget Best-of-$N$ selector that ranks sampled rollouts by cross-view depth reprojection consistency of their predicted futures, computed with a frozen geometry foundation model. \methodgated\ adds a lightweight action--future consistency gate that invokes \method\ only when the initial rollout appears internally inconsistent. Across five benchmark--backbone settings on RoboCasa, LIBERO Long, and RoboTwin~2.0, fixed-budget \method\ improves $N{=}8$ task success in every setting, e.g., raising the RoboCasa group average from $66.3\%$ to $68.4\%$ with Cosmos Policy and from $80.8\%$ to $82.5\%$ with X-WAM. With gating enabled, \methodgated\ recovers on average $74.8\%$ of the always-on success gain while triggering additional sampling on only $26.2\%$ of decision points. Offline diagnostics show that cross-view reprojection is a strong task-label-free selector, and we identify false low-score selections as a failure mode that helps explain why performance can saturate or degrade as $N$ increases.

Patch Policy: Efficient Embodied Control via Dense Visual Representations
2026-07-20 cs.RO, cs.LG Top author: Gaoyue Zhou (h=9)
Gaoyue Zhou, Zichen Jeff Cui, Ada Langford, Bowen Tan, Yann LeCun
Core Contributions
  • Argues current robot policies waste pretrained ViT features by pooling them into one global token or training vision backbones from scratch, rather than consuming the dense patch-level detail large-scale visual pretraining already provides.
  • Introduces a block-causal attention mask letting a transformer policy attend over many dense patch tokens per observation while preserving temporal causality, avoiding the cost of a full billion-parameter VLM backbone that large VLAs typically inherit.
  • Achieves a 40% relative improvement over global-pooled representation baselines across four simulated and three real-world suites, and beats fine-tuned OpenVLA-OFT by 18% while using roughly 0.7% of its parameters.
  • Positions itself as a lightweight bridge letting the robotics community exploit continuing progress in visual representation learning without sacrificing the inference speed high-frequency reactive control requires.
Show abstract

Pretrained dense visual features from Vision Transformers (ViTs) are powerful yet have been underutilized in robot learning. Modern robot policies either compress each observation into a single global token, or rely on visual backbones trained from scratch, sacrificing both fine-grained spatial detail and the benefits of large-scale visual pre-training. While there exist policies that do operate on dense patch features like large vision-language-action models (VLAs), they tend to be heavy and slow, inheriting the full cost of a billion-parameter vision-language model (VLM) backbone. We close this gap with Patch Policy, a minimal architectural extension that enables transformer-based policies to consume dense pre-trained patch tokens directly without the computational overhead of a full VLM. At its core is a block-causal attention mask that preserves the temporal causality of standard policies while letting the model attend over many patch tokens per observation, alongside other state information. Patch Policy is lightweight, fast, and highly effective. Across four simulated and three real-world environment suites, our method achieves a 40% relative improvement over policies using state-of-the-art global-pooled representations. Furthermore, it surpasses fine-tuned OpenVLA-OFT by 18% while using roughly 0.7% of the parameters. We believe Patch Policy provides a pipeline for the robotics community to readily leverage continuing progress in visual representation learning, without sacrificing the training efficiency or inference speed required for high-frequency, reactive control. Videos can be viewed at https://patch-policy.github.io

GeoWorldAD: Geometry World Action Model for Autonomous Driving
2026-07-20 cs.RO Top author: Guang Chen (h=8)
Songyan Zhang, Jinyuan Tian, Hanbing Li, Daqi Liu, Hao Chen
Core Contributions
  • Targets a specific limitation of Vision/Video-Action driving models: they scale with better vision transformers and data but lack explicit geometric grounding, defaulting toward overly conservative collision avoidance because they can't reason about how free space will evolve.
  • Grounds trajectory planning in ego-aligned 3D space using present geometry for spatial constraints while adding latent future-geometry tokens that anticipate short-horizon scene evolution, letting the model be less conservative without sacrificing safety.
  • Progressively aggregates multi-scale present and future geometry through iterative trajectory refinement rather than a single-shot geometric encoding.
  • Reports state-of-the-art performance on both NAVSIM v1 and v2, evidence that explicit geometric grounding plus future-geometry modeling outperforms purely vision/video-conditioned action generation for driving.
Show abstract

Autonomous driving requires both safe and efficient planning decisions in dynamic 3D environments. Although recent Vision/Video-Action models learn policies directly from visual observations and scale well with advances in vision transformers and large-scale training data, they often lack explicit geometric grounding and future-aware spatial guidance, limiting their ability to balance collision avoidance and driving progress. In this work, we propose GeoWorldAD, a geometry world action model that grounds trajectory planning in ego-aligned 3D space and anticipates short-horizon scene evolution with latent future geometry tokens. Present geometry provides essential spatial constraints for safe planning, while future geometry reveals how surrounding agents and ego-centric free space may evolve, reducing overly conservative decisions without sacrificing safety. To efficiently exploit these geometric cues, GeoWorldAD progressively aggregates multi-scale present geometry and latent future geometry through iterative trajectory refinement. Experiments on NAVSIM v1 and v2 demonstrate state-of-the-art performance, highlighting the effectiveness of explicit 3D geometry grounding and future geometry world modeling for safe and efficient autonomous driving.

Closing the Loop in Humanoid VLA: Persistent 3D Object Tokens for Verifiable Loco-Manipulation
2026-07-20 cs.RO Top author: Haoyang Ge (h=1)
Peng Ren, Haoyang Ge, Jiang Zhao, Cong Huang, Yukun Shi
Core Contributions
  • Names a specific failure mode in long-horizon humanoid loco-manipulation as "object-state divergence": the object state used to condition an action can differ from the state used to judge whether the action succeeded, since VLA policies typically don't track objects as persistent entities across movement, contact, occlusion, and recovery.
  • Proposes Persistent Object Tokenization (POT), maintaining role-indexed 3D object records from RGB-D observations and converting them into tokens that both condition the whole-body action expert and support geometric predicate checks — the same representation drives action generation and verification.
  • On a Unitree G1, POT-VLA improves a matched GR00T-N1.7 baseline from 39/80 to 71/80 successes across eight real-world task families — nearly doubling raw success with the same base policy.
  • Achieves 44/50 on aligned Being-0 service tasks versus the 37/50 reported by the original Being-0 paper, with the largest gains concentrated on tasks requiring maintained 3D relations — evidence persistent object state, not just a better base policy, drives the improvement.
Show abstract

Vision-language-action policies are a promising foundation for general robot control, but long-horizon humanoid loco-manipulation requires the robot to treat task objects as persistent physical entities across movement, contact, occlusion, and recovery. We study this problem as object-state divergence: the object state used to condition a whole-body action can differ from the state used to decide whether the action achieved the intended physical relation. We propose \emph{Persistent Object Tokenization} (POT), which maintains role-indexed 3D object records from RGB-D observations and converts them into object tokens for a whole-body action expert. Instantiated as \emph{POT-VLA}, the same object records condition action generation and support geometric predicate checks, yielding a closed-loop execution system in which object state is both actionable and verifiable. On a Unitree G1, POT-VLA improves a matched direct GR00T-N1.7 baseline from 39/80 to 71/80 successes over eight real-world task families. In an external Being-0-aligned reference, POT-VLA achieves 44/50 successes on aligned service tasks, compared with the 37/50 success reported by the Being-0 paper. The largest gains occur on tasks requiring maintained 3D relations, suggesting that persistent object-centered state is a useful abstraction for verifiable humanoid VLA execution.

RoboHarness: Memory-Driven Orchestration of Heterogeneous Robot Policies for Long-Horizon Planning
2026-07-20 cs.RO Top author: Jinbang Huang (h=0)
Jinbang Huang, Yuanzhao Hu, Zhiyuan Li, Ran Qi, Yixin Xiao
Core Contributions
  • Targets long-horizon tasks no single policy handles well by encapsulating independently developed control systems — VLAs, RL policies, TAMP systems — as reusable "agentic skills" within one orchestration framework, rather than building another homogeneous skill library with fixed applicability.
  • Characterizes each policy's capability boundaries using multi-modal execution memory and online evidence, enabling capability-aware decomposition and routing instead of assuming a fixed, predefined skill set.
  • Introduces a "Memory Bridge" that retrieves past execution trajectories for the next policy in a handoff, estimates its in-distribution state region, and steers the robot there — stabilizing handoffs between heterogeneous policies without joint retraining.
  • Demonstrates effective routing and stable orchestration across three public benchmarks, 500 customized tasks, and 135 real-robot experiments, with substantial gains in zero-shot long-horizon planning and out-of-distribution robustness.
Show abstract

Long-horizon robotic tasks require diverse capabilities that no single policy can reliably provide. Heterogeneous policies offer complementary strengths, but orchestrating them requires reasoning over uncertain capability boundaries and cross-policy distribution mismatch, which are largely overlooked by existing planning methods built on homogeneous, predefined skills with fixed applicability. We propose RoboHarness, a unified framework that encapsulates independently developed robot control systems as reusable agentic skills. Although instantiated in this work with VLAs, RL policies, and task-and-motion planning (TAMP) systems, RoboHarness is designed as a general framework compatible with a broader range of robot policies, such as navigation policies, model predictive controllers, and world-action models. RoboHarness uses multi-modal execution memory and online evidence to characterize policy capability boundaries for capability-aware decomposition and routing. To stabilize policy handoffs, its Memory Bridge retrieves execution trajectories associated with the next policy, estimates its in-distribution state region, and guides the robot toward that region without joint policy retraining. Extensive experiments on three public benchmarks, 500 customized tasks, and 135 real-robot experiments demonstrate effective capability-aware routing and stable policy orchestration, yielding substantial improvements in zero-shot long-horizon planning and out-of-distribution robustness.

HCPG-Flow:Hierarchical Contact-Progress Guidance for Flow-Policy Robot Manipulation
2026-07-20 cs.RO Top author: Yang Liu (h=4)
Guanghu Xie, Mingxu Li, Shuo Zhang, Yonglong Zhang, Yifan Yang
Core Contributions
  • Addresses a structural mismatch in flow-policy manipulation: multiple action proposals can be sampled per step, but critic-based ranking ties data collection to value estimates that may be poorly represented in the replay buffer — this adds an analytic, rollout-time selector instead.
  • Introduces hierarchical, object-centric contact-progress guidance that switches from end-effector-approach scoring to task-progress scoring specifically after contact, standardizing proposal scores and executing via a temperature-controlled action embedding, while preserving SAC-Flow's original actor/critic objectives.
  • Improves mean success over SAC-Flow across ten simulated tasks on two benchmarks, including a 9.5 percentage-point gain on Maniskill.
  • On four physical tasks, cuts successful completion steps by 17.4%, showing the contact-progress signal improves trajectory efficiency, not just success rate.
Show abstract

Flow policies can represent multimodal action distributions for robot manipulation, yet a robot must execute one action at each control step. When several proposals are sampled, critic-based ranking makes data collection depend on value estimates over candidate actions that may be weakly represented in replay. We introduce HCPG-Flow, an analytic rollout-time selector that augments SAC-Flow with hierarchical, object-centric contact-progress guidance while preserving its actor and critic objectives. HCPG switches from end-effector approach to task progress after contact, scores each proposal by the first-order reduction of a task-relevant distance, standardizes scores within the candidate set, and executes a temperature-controlled action embedding. Across ten simulated tasks, HCPG improves mean success over SAC-Flow on both benchmarks, including a 9.5 percentage-point gain on Maniskill. FM-VLA: Force-based Memory for Vision-Language-Action Models in Contact-Rich Manipulation
2026-07-20 cs.RO Top author: Zhiying Du (h=3)
Ruicheng Li, Qixiu Li, Ruichun Ma, Yu Deng, Lin Luo
Core Contributions
  • Targets a blind spot in memory-augmented VLA models: existing vision-based memory conditions on past image frames, which is expensive and fails precisely when temporal events are visually ambiguous, e.g. repeated small-motion button presses that look nearly identical across frames.
  • Replaces vision-based history with force-based memory: force histories are encoded into compact tokens via a VAE pretrained on force time-series reconstruction, then projected alongside short state history as extra conditioning for the action expert — swapping an expensive visual channel for a cheaper, more informative one.
  • Evaluated specifically on tasks requiring non-Markovian temporal reasoning (finding a hidden block, counting button presses, counting wipes) rather than generic manipulation benchmarks, directly testing the memory mechanism itself.
  • Achieves over 80% success on these memory-dependent tasks with minimal inference overhead, significantly outperforming baseline approaches.
Show abstract

Vision-language-action (VLA) models have achieved impressive generalization in robotic manipulation, and recent memory-augmented VLAs have relaxed the Markovian assumption by conditioning on past images or language summaries. Vision-based memory approaches address this by conditioning on sampled past image frames, but they are computationally expensive and fundamentally limited when temporal events are visually ambiguous, e.g., pushing a button multiple times with small movements. We propose FM-VLA, a VLA model with force-based memory, enabling temporal context reasoning for non-Markovian, contact-rich manipulation. We encode force histories into compact force memory tokens with a variational autoencoder (VAE) pretrained with force time series reconstruction. By projecting force latent representations and short state history as additional conditioning tokens to the action expert module, we enable VLAs to leverage accumulated contact event history to guide manipulation. We evaluate FM-VLA on three memory-dependent tasks, including finding a hidden block, pressing a button, and wiping a dish for a specific number of times. Our lightweight force memory achieves over 80% success rate with minimal inference overhead, significantly outperforming baseline approaches. Project page: https://qft-333.github.io/FM-VLA-Page/

2026-07-20 cs.RO, cs.AI Top author: Hye Jung Yoon (h=2)

  • Pushes back against end-to-end learned bin-picking, which falters on unfamiliar or complex objects, by decomposing suction grasping into three explicit modules — segmentation, grasping, classification — rather than one learned mapping.

Show abstract

Current bin picking methods that rely heavily on end-to-end learning often falter when confronted with unfamiliar or complex objects in unstructured environments. To overcome these limitations, we introduce Seg2Grasp, a modular pipeline designed for robust suction grasping in dynamic and cluttered bin scenarios. Seg2Grasp is built on a three-step process: Segmentation, Grasping, and Classification. The Segmentation module employs a Transformer-based model to generate class-agnostic object masks from RGB-D images, ensuring accurate detection across various conditions. The Grasping module uses surface normals and mask proposals to determine the optimal suction points, enhancing grasp success. Finally, the Classification module leverages fine-tuned open-vocabulary Mask-CLIP for precise object identification, enabling versatile handling of diverse objects. Real-world robotic experiments demonstrate that Seg2Grasp outperforms existing methods in success rates and adaptability, establishing it as a powerful tool for automated bin picking in industrial settings.

Manifold-Guided Motion Planning for Tight Assemblies
2026-07-20 cs.RO Top author: Dror Livnat (h=0)
Dror Livnat, Michael M. Bilevich, Michal Kleinbort, Dan Halperin
Core Contributions
  • Targets rigid-body assembly motions that must pass through near-zero-clearance configurations, where standard sampling-based planners struggle because valid paths occupy a vanishingly small fraction of configuration space.
  • Observes that in tight assemblies, solution paths concentrate near a "critical manifold" — configurations with at least one contact point between parts — and biases sampling toward that manifold's neighborhood via hierarchical configuration-space subdivision, rather than sampling uniformly.
  • Proves CMG-RRT is probabilistically complete under standard clearance assumptions, giving a theoretical guarantee alongside the practical sampling bias.
  • Achieves a 100% success rate across all tested rotational-assembly benchmarks, including reportedly the first fully automatic solution to the Elk disentanglement puzzle — a hard instance used as an informal difficulty marker in the field.
Show abstract

Motion planning for rigid-body assembly poses a fundamental challenge in robotics due to tight geometric constraints. In such scenarios, feasible motions often require passing through (near-)zero clearance configurations in which the parts are tightly constrained by contact. In this work, we introduce Critical-Manifold Guided RRT (CMG-RRT), a sampling-based planner designed specifically for tight assembly problems. Our key observation is that in tight assemblies, valid solution paths lie on or near a critical manifold: the subset of configuration space consisting of poses with at least one contact point between parts. CMG-RRT guides exploration by adaptively biasing sampling toward neighborhoods of the critical manifold using a hierarchical subdivision of the configuration space. We prove that CMG-RRT is probabilistically complete under standard clearance assumptions. Empirical evaluation on challenging rotational assembly benchmarks demonstrates a 100% success rate across all tested instances, including, to the best of our knowledge, the first fully automatic solution of the Elk disentanglement puzzle. Our open source software is available through our project page: https://www.cgl.cs.tau.ac.il/projects/tight-assembly-planning.

We provide a novel end-to-end framework for the execution of an assembly operation by two robotic arms, given the digital CAD models of the parts and their desired relative placement in their assembled state.
