Research Landscape

The dominant thread in today's batch is escaping the expensive robot-teleoperation loop for manipulation skill acquisition, attacked from several independent angles. EgoRecovery substitutes egocentric human video for robot teleoperation to collect failure-recovery data, reporting over 10x more valid recovery data per hour once aligned to a shared corrective-intent space. HOST goes further, acquiring an entirely new skill at inference time from a single human video in 29 seconds via a self-grounded progress-prediction cascade, with no gradient updates and no erosion of previously mastered skills. LENS and SeededGrasp take a complementary, architectural route: rather than scaling one monolithic policy, both decouple high-level semantic reasoning (an LLM scene-abstraction layer, a VLM seed-point predictor) from low-level geometric execution (existing planners, a lightweight grasp model), avoiding costly joint end-to-end training. DEED pushes back against the assumption that this is fundamentally an architecture problem at all — its central claim is that closing the lab-to-store gap for a retail VLA humanoid is a systems-integration challenge, solvable with careful data curation and post-training on a single GPU rather than a new model.

A second cluster tackles heterogeneity in navigation and perception — of embodiment, context, and model scale — largely through self-supervision and decoupling rather than brute-force scaling. DINS-IO learns inertial odometry with no position labels at all, using the strapdown INS velocity recursion itself as a differentiable consistency loss. EA-Nav injects embodiment geometry as explicit conditioning tokens to resolve the ambiguity of identical visual input implying different valid actions for different robot bodies. NavVerse exposes a concrete adaptation bottleneck by forcing a single continuous episode to cross from indoors to outdoors, finding today's agents drop sharply in that transition even when they handle each context well in isolation. SOPD-SocialNav distills social-navigation reasoning from a large VLM into a lightweight student using entropy-gated token selection, while the Disjoint Parameter Training paper diagnoses a related "Skill Conflict" — shared encoders forcing prediction and planning to compete for the same weights — and resolves it by separating, then sparsely merging, task-specific parameters.

A third, quieter theme is a push toward formal and geometric rigor in places that used to rely on empirical validation alone. Contact-Persistent Full Actuation proves that a UAV's ability to sustain contact is exactly equivalent to the task wrench sitting inside a feasible-wrench polytope, turning a control property into a checkable geometric certificate. KineBench removes a confounding Inverse Dynamics Model from closed-loop world-model evaluation entirely, and Koopman Dreamer derives an explicit multi-step rollout-error bound that separates spectral-backbone amplification from stochastic mismatch. Courteous Anticipation and the reconfiguring-boats paper bring similar rigor to multi-robot coordination, via factored future-cost estimators and CBF-based safety filters respectively. Taken together, the batch reads less like a race toward bigger end-to-end models and more like a field consolidating around decoupled, self-supervised, and provably-bounded components wherever a monolithic black box previously stood in.

EgoRecovery: Acquiring Failure Recovery Ability Through Human Recovery Demonstration

2026-07-22 cs.RO, cs.CV Xiaosong Jia (h=23)
Zuhao Ge, Yuchen Zhou, Weitao Zhou, Minglei Li, Xinyu Li
Core Contributions
  • Targets the recovery-data bottleneck directly: recovery demonstrations are far more diverse than success demonstrations, so teleoperation-based collection can't scale — the paper substitutes egocentric human video for robot teleoperation.
  • Reports over 10x the valid recovery data per hour from human operators versus robot teleoperation under their protocol, since inducing a failure and demonstrating a correction is far faster for a human than resetting and re-teleoperating a robot.
  • Bridges the embodiment gap via a shared "corrective-intent space" — human and robot recovery segments are aligned to a compact representation capturing timing and magnitude of correction, rather than requiring pixel-perfect human-robot visual alignment.
  • Only a small number of robot recovery demos are needed to connect this shared intent space to executable actions, and a learned recovery gate decides at deployment when to activate corrective behavior.
  • Beats robot-only recovery training, naive co-training with human data, and direct intent-transfer baselines on real-world recovery tasks.
Show abstract

Robust embodied robots should be able to recover from failures and retry tasks in order to operate reliably in unstructured and noisy real-world environments. Achieving this capability requires training policies on data that captures recovery behaviors. However, collecting such data through robot teleoperation is difficult to scale, as it is time-consuming to induce diverse failure states, perform corrective actions, and reset the environment. This challenge is further exacerbated by the high diversity of failure modes, which demands substantially more recovery data than success demonstrations. In this work, we show that egocentric human data capturing failure recovery processes provides a scalable alternative. By efficiently arranging task-level failure configurations and recording short recovery segments, human operators can generate more than 10x as much valid recovery data per hour compared to robot teleoperation under our protocol. To address the embodiment gap between human and robot, we propose EgoRecovery, a co-training framework for learning recovery behavior, where human recovery demonstrations are aligned to a compact corrective-intent space shared with robot data, which captures the timing and magnitude of the correction. Only a small number of robot recovery demonstrations are required to connect this intent to executable robot actions. At deployment, a learned recovery gate predicts when correction is needed from robot observations and activates the corrective intent only in recovery states. Experiments on real-world recovery tasks show that EgoRecovery improves success from failure starts over robot-only recovery, direct co-training with human recovery data, and direct intent-transfer baselines.

LENS: LLM-guided Environment Simplification for Planning and Control in Clutter

2026-07-22 cs.RO Rachel Holladay (h=14)
Aileen Liao, Rachel Holladay, Dinesh Jayaraman, Michael Posa
Core Contributions
  • Rather than training a bigger end-to-end policy to handle clutter, LENS keeps existing planning/control/VLA stacks intact and auto-generates a simplified scene abstraction on top of them, sidestepping the manual, task-specific engineering scene abstractions have historically required.
  • The LLM-guided simplification runs in closed loop with task progress, merging stacked objects and pruning distant distractors adaptively as manipulation proceeds, rather than committing to one fixed abstraction upfront.
  • Framed explicitly as "plug-and-play": the same abstraction layer improves three different downstream approaches — classical planning, model-based control, and a vision-language-action model — evidence the benefit comes from the abstraction itself, not a method-specific integration.
  • Validated across a diverse set of highly cluttered manipulation scenes, targeting exactly the distractor-heavy, contact-uncertain settings where general-purpose manipulation approaches degrade.
Show abstract

Despite recent advances in general-purpose robotic manipulation, real-world multi-object clutter remains challenging to handle for today's prevalent approaches. The problem scales in complexity due to more objects and collisions, more unpredictable contact physics, distractors, and task ambiguity. Bridging this gap to real-world deployment requires effective scene abstractions; yet today, producing such abstractions requires extensive task-specific manual engineering, which does not scale. These abstractions are costly to generate and difficult to adjust or fine-tune. We instead propose a plug-and-play fix to automatically generate scene-specific, task-specific, adaptively updating abstractions on top of existing planning and control stacks. LLM-guided Environment Simplification (LENS) produces a de-cluttered abstracted scene representation by merging (e.g., stacked objects) or pruning (e.g., distant objects) scene entities in a closed loop in response to task progress. These dynamic, task-relevant abstractions are versatile and easy to use. In our experiments, we show that LENS improves classical planning, model-based control, and a vision-language-action model, across a diverse set of highly cluttered manipulation scenes. Project website: https://lens-2026.github.io/.

Robots Acquire Manipulation Skills in Seconds from a Single Human Video

2026-07-22 cs.RO Yufeng Yue (h=12)
Guangyan Chen, Meiling Wang, Te Cui, Zichen Zhou, Qi Shao
Core Contributions
  • Attacks the same "cumbersome training loop" problem as EgoRecovery from a different angle: HOST acquires a brand-new skill at inference time from one human video, with no gradient updates that would risk eroding previously mastered skills.
  • Works via a "self-grounded prediction" cascade: estimate the robot's progress within the demonstrated task, translate that progression into the robot's own predicted future observations, then derive actions from those predicted observations.
  • Training targets come from mapping both the robot trajectory and the human video onto a shared task-progress manifold, then redefining targets to match the video's future progression — avoiding the need for paired human-robot demonstrations of the same skill.
  • Acquires novel skills in an average of 29 seconds with 62% success, beating a zero-shot baseline by 45 points while retaining old skills, and matches a baseline fine-tuned on 50 robot demos per task while needing 50x fewer demonstrations and 507x less acquisition time.
Show abstract

The ability to acquire skills rapidly and effortlessly while retaining those already mastered is essential for robots. However, current methods still rely on a cumbersome training-time loop that is costly and slow, while eroding skills already mastered. In this paper, we introduce HOST (Human-to-robot One-Shot Skill AcquisiTion), a framework that enables a robot to acquire skills in seconds from a single human video while retaining previously mastered skills. HOST resolves skill acquisition through a cascade of self-grounded prediction. It first estimates the robot's progress within the demonstrated task, then translates the upcoming progression into the robot's own future observations, and finally derives actions from these predicted observations. This cascade is trained on targets coupled to the video demonstration, obtained by mapping the robot trajectory and the video demonstration onto a shared task progress manifold, then redefining each target to align with the future progression of the video. HOST thereby enables the robot to actively follow the demonstrated procedure and adapt it to the robot's embodiment. HOST acquires novel skills at inference time from a single human video in an average of 29 seconds and achieves a 62% average success rate. It exceeds the zero-shot baseline by 45% while retaining previously mastered skills. HOST even exceeds the baseline fine-tuned on 50 robot demonstrations per task while requiring 50 times fewer demonstrations and acquiring each skill 507 times faster. Additional information about HOST is available on the project website.

SeededGrasp: Language-Guided Grasping in Complex Scenes with Multiple Embodiments

2026-07-22 cs.RO Maria Attarian (h=7)
Yang Xu, Gurpreet Singh Mukker, Raymond Wang, Jasper Gerigk, Maria Attarian
Core Contributions
  • Splits the two failure modes of current VLM-based grasping cleanly apart: predicting the grasp directly loses spatial precision, while training the VLM jointly with the grasp model needs far more data/compute — SeededGrasp instead has the VLM predict a seed point and hands geometric execution to a separate lightweight model.
  • This decoupling of high-level semantic reasoning from low-level geometric execution is what enables multi-embodiment support without expensive end-to-end retraining per embodiment.
  • Releases the first multi-embodiment tabletop grasping dataset at 2.5M grasps in cluttered scenes, a scale specifically built to support this seed-point-conditioned architecture rather than a generic grasping corpus.
  • Reports 72% success in simulation and 78% in real-world grasping, outperforming existing baselines — notably higher in the real world than in sim, suggesting the seed-point interface transfers cleanly rather than exploiting simulation-specific shortcuts.
Show abstract

Practical robotic grasping in complex scenes requires both 3D spatial reasoning and alignment with task-specific requirements. Vision-language models (VLMs) offer a natural way to specify these requirements using language, but existing approaches either use a VLM to predict the grasp directly with limited spatial awareness, or train the VLM together with the grasping model, which requires significantly more data and compute. These limitations impede performance and have prevented scaling to multiple embodiments in complex scenes. We address this by proposing SeededGrasp, a novel data-efficient framework that enables a VLM to predict a seed point to be used as conditioning for a subsequent lightweight grasp-generation model. Our architecture decouples high-level semantic reasoning from low-level geometric execution, enabling multi-embodiment support while bypassing the need for expensive end-to-end training. To enable training such models, we release the first multi-embodiment tabletop grasping dataset comprising over 2.5M grasps in cluttered scenes. Experimental results demonstrate that our approach outperforms existing baselines, achieving 72% success in simulation and 78% in real-world grasping experiments. See our project site for data and code: https://uoft-isl.github.io/seeded-grasp/

Closing the Lab-to-Store Gap: A Data-Efficient Post-Training and Experience-Driven Learning VLA Framework for Retail Humanoids

2026-07-22 cs.RO, cs.AI Tiago Silvério (h=4)
Roger Sala Sisó, Tiago Silvério, Jakob Sand, Tran Nguyen Le
Core Contributions
  • Its central claim is unusual and worth flagging directly: closing the gap between benchmark VLA performance and reliable real-world retail deployment is a systems-integration challenge, not an architectural one — a naively fine-tuned GR00T N1.6 policy fails in-store, but careful data curation and targeted post-training turn it into a working system on a single GPU.
  • The post-training pipeline combines control-frequency alignment, data curation, task-relevant visual highlighting, and reduced dependence on the VLA backbone itself — a bundle of practical fixes rather than one core algorithmic change.
  • Adapts RECAP-style experience-driven refinement to a real supermarket chip-restocking task via a text-based advantage prefix and a vision-language value function, letting the system improve from its own execution experience rather than only curated demonstrations.
  • Adds a latent-space analysis tool specifically to study in- versus out-of-distribution behavior, giving a diagnostic for why a given fine-tuning choice helped or hurt, rather than only reporting aggregate success rates.
Show abstract

Closing the gap between benchmark performance and reliable real-world operation remains a central challenge for Vision-Language-Action (VLA) humanoid robots, which must handle execution errors, distribution shifts, and environmental variability. This paper presents DEED (Data-Efficient Post-Training and Experience-Driven Learning), a systems-level approach evaluated on a supermarket chip-restocking task using a Unitree G1-Edu humanoid robot and the GR00T N1.6 foundation model. DEED comprises three key components: (1) a data-efficient post-training pipeline with control-frequency alignment, data curation, task-relevant visual highlighting, and reduced VLA dependence; (2) a real-world study of experience-driven refinement, adapted from RECAP via a text-based advantage prefix and a vision-language value function; and (3) a latent-space analysis tool for studying in- and out-of-distribution behavior. Our results suggest that bridging the lab-to-store gap is primarily a systems integration challenge rather than an architectural one: careful data design and targeted post-training can transform a policy that fails under naive fine-tuning into a competent real-world system using only a single GPU.

V2F: Vision-Informed Grasp Force Prediction for Damage-Aware Robotic Handling of Date Fruits

2026-07-22 cs.RO Eric Feron (h=4)
Shahd Shami, Obadah Wali, Eric Feron, Shinkyu Park
Core Contributions
  • Date fruits present a narrow safe-handling window — high detachment force needed but low bruise threshold — so the paper first runs mechanical characterization to quantify the relationship between fruit geometry and bioyield stress, establishing a safe grasping envelope before any learning happens.
  • The Vision-to-Force pipeline predicts safe grasp force from non-contact visual descriptors (segmentation, active-contour refinement, geometric features) plus cultivar metadata, avoiding slow, potentially damaging tactile exploration to estimate the same quantity.
  • Uses a physics-informed residual neural network that augments a Hertz contact equation rather than a black-box regressor, so the geometric/mechanical prior does most of the work and the network only corrects residual error.
  • Achieves R² ≈ 0.7 on unseen cultivar groups and validates with a real gripper and load cell, keeping residual deformation under 1 mm with no observable damage — a modest but honestly reported fit given the acknowledged mechanical variability of biological tissue.
Show abstract

This paper presents a vision-informed grasp force prediction framework for robotic handling of date fruits. Addressing the dual challenge of high detachment forces and low bruise thresholds, we first conduct mechanical characterization on date samples to define a safe grasping envelope and quantify the relationship between fruit geometry and bioyield stress. In this work, we develop a Vision-to-Force (V2F) pipeline that combines computer vision-based segmentation, active-contour refinement, and geometric feature extraction with a physics-informed residual neural network that augments a Hertz contact equation. The resulting model maps non-contact visual descriptors and cultivar metadata to predict a safe grasp force with mean validation performance of R² ≈ 0.7 across unseen cultivar groups, which is a good result given the inherent mechanical variability of biological tissue. Experimental validation using a gripper and load cell indicates that the predicted forces enable stable manipulation of different types of date fruits, with residual deformations below 1 mm and no observable damage. These results show that pre-emptive, vision-driven force estimation can replace slow and potentially damaging tactile exploration, enabling safer robotic handling of fragile fruits.

Diffusion ReRoll: Revisable Denoising for Robotic Sequential Prediction

2026-07-22 cs.RO, cs.LG Seongil Hong (h=3)
Seonsoo Kim, Seongil Hong, Jun-Gill Kang
Core Contributions
  • Standard diffusion-based sequence predictors commit to a single monotonic denoising pass; Diffusion ReRoll instead selectively re-noises regions that have become locally "stable" while the rest of the horizon keeps denoising, letting settled segments be revised again using newer context.
  • This structured re-noising gives iterative cross-horizon revision — earlier and later parts of a plan can revise each other — that plain forward-only diffusion or causal Diffusion-Forcing-style denoising cannot express.
  • Reports concrete relative gains across three distinct settings: 21% over Diffusion Forcing in guidance-based planning and 23% over Diffuser in goal-inpainting on OGBench maze tasks, plus a 56.5% average success improvement over Diffusion Policy on LIBERO-10.
  • The largest relative gains appear specifically under out-of-distribution evaluation in the unified video-action setting, suggesting the revision mechanism's main value is correcting for distribution shift rather than just polishing in-distribution predictions.
Show abstract

We propose Diffusion ReRoll, a diffusion-based framework for robotic sequential prediction that enables revisable denoising over horizons. Existing diffusion-based sequence predictors typically perform a single monotonic denoising process. In contrast, Diffusion ReRoll selectively re-noises regions that have become locally stable while the remaining regions continue denoising, so the re-noised regions can be refined again using context from the rest of the horizon. This structured re-noising enables iterative cross-horizon revision, allowing earlier and later segments to revise one another, while maintaining local consistency. We evaluate Diffusion ReRoll against full-sequence diffusion and causal denoising based on Diffusion Forcing across long-horizon planning, policy learning, and unified video-action modeling. On OGBench PointMaze and AntMaze, Diffusion ReRoll achieves relative gains in average success rate of 21% over Diffusion Forcing in matched guidance-based planning and 23% over Diffuser in matched goal-inpainting. In diffusion-policy-style action prediction, Diffusion ReRoll improves average success by 56.5% relative to Diffusion Policy across different prediction horizons and history lengths on the LIBERO-10 multi-task benchmark. In unified video-action prediction, Diffusion ReRoll improves policy and inverse dynamics performance, especially under out-of-distribution evaluation, and achieves the best action-video consistency. These results support structured re-noising as an effective mechanism for revisable robotic sequence generation.

DINS-IO: Learned Inertial Odometry via Differentiable INS Consistency

2026-07-22 cs.RO Jian Kuang (h=21)
Hao Qiao, Yan Wang, Jian Kuang, Xiaoji Niu
Core Contributions
  • Removes the dependency on expensive motion-capture/VIO/SLAM position ground truth that current learned inertial odometry needs, by exploiting the strapdown INS velocity recursion itself as a differentiable self-supervision signal.
  • The consistency constraint — predicted velocity rotated into the nav frame must match integrated specific force up to unknown initial velocity and a constant bias — is solved in closed form, and the solver's own residual becomes the training loss, so gradients flow through an analytic solution rather than a black box.
  • A high-frequency network emits dense body-frame velocity at IMU rate to supply this per-sample constraint, contrasting with lower-rate position-supervised approaches.
  • Since self-supervision alone yields motion that's consistent but not metrically calibrated, the model is calibrated to true scale using only a few labeled trajectories via low-rank (LoRA) adaptation.
  • Matches or surpasses fully supervised baselines on standard benchmarks despite using only a small fraction of position labels for fine-tuning.
Show abstract

The training of learned inertial odometry depends on dense, high-precision position ground truth from motion capture, visual-inertial odometry or SLAM, which is costly and hard to acquire at scale. We propose DINS-IO, which learns inertial odometry directly from raw IMU streams without position labels. Our key insight is that the strapdown INS velocity recursion is a strong, fully differentiable consistency prior: the predicted velocity, rotated into the navigation frame, must agree with the integrated specific force up to an unknown initial velocity and a constant accelerometer bias. We cast this constraint as a sliding-window least-squares problem with a globally shared bias, solve it in closed form, and use the solver residual as a self-supervised loss whose gradient flows back to the network through the analytic solution. To supply this per-sample constraint, we design a high-frequency network that emits dense body-frame velocity at the IMU rate. Since the self-supervised network learns consistent motion but its velocity is not yet metrically calibrated, we calibrate it to true metric velocity from a few labeled trajectories by directly supervising the predicted body-frame velocity and adapting only low-rank (LoRA) patches. On standard benchmarks, DINS-IO pretrained self-supervised and fine-tuned with a small fraction of labels matches or surpasses fully supervised baselines.

ReferTrack: Referring Then Tracking for Embodied Visual Tracking

2026-07-22 cs.RO Jiazhao Zhang (h=19)
Hanjing Ye, Tianle Zeng, Jiazhao Zhang, Shaoan Wang, Zibo Zhang
Core Contributions
  • Splits embodied visual tracking into an explicit "refer, then track" pipeline instead of folding target identification into an opaque VLA chain-of-thought that operates in abstract spatial latents hard to supervise or align with image-space detections.
  • Grounds target selection in an indexed set of bounding boxes so identification is directly supervisable, then decodes tracking waypoints conditioned on that concrete image-grounded choice.
  • Maintains a sliding-window queue of prior selected boxes and injects their geometry into visual history via temporal-viewpoint-bbox indicator tokens, preserving target motion cues a single-frame detection would lose.
  • Achieves 89.4% / 73.3% / 74.1% success on the single-target, distracted, and ambiguity splits of EVT-Bench with only a single forward-facing camera — matching or beating several multi-camera baselines — and transfers zero-shot to real legged and humanoid platforms.
Show abstract

Embodied visual tracking (EVT) requires a mobile agent to continuously follow a specific target described in natural language using only onboard vision. While recent vision-language-action (VLA) policies unify target identification and trajectory planning, their chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning often operates in abstract spatial latents that are difficult to supervise and weakly aligned with explicit image-space detections. To address this, we introduce ReferTrack, a referring-then-tracking paradigm that grounds EVT using a single forward-facing camera. Our model first selects the target from an indexed set of bounding boxes, then decodes tracking waypoints conditioned on this image-grounded decision. To preserve target motion cues over time, ReferTrack maintains a sliding-window queue of previously selected bounding boxes, injecting their geometric features into the visual history via temporal-viewpoint-bbox indicator (TVBI) tokens. We further enhance target identification by co-training on a custom Refer-QA dataset. On EVT-Bench, ReferTrack achieves state-of-the-art single-view performance with success rates of 89.4%, 73.3%, and 74.1% on the single-target, distracted, and ambiguity tracking splits, respectively -- matching or even surpassing several multi-camera baselines on identification-heavy tasks. Finally, real-world deployments on legged and humanoid robots validate its robust sim-to-real transfer capabilities. Code is available at https://github.com/MedlarTea/referTrack.

NavVerse: Benchmarking Indoor-to-Outdoor Embodied Navigation in Continuous Robot Simulation

2026-07-22 cs.RO, cs.CV Maani Ghaffari (h=17)
Junzhe Wu, Yue Hu, Zeyu Han, Po-Hsun Chang, Yinan Dong
Core Contributions
  • Existing navigation benchmarks split indoor and outdoor evaluation and often abstract away robot execution; NavVerse instead forces a single continuous episode to cross from inside a building to the street, exposing exit-finding, boundary-traversal, and kinodynamic failure modes separate benchmarks hide.
  • Provides 100 indoor, 50 outdoor, and 50 indoor-to-outdoor physics-enabled scenes across 10,000 episodes spanning Object Navigation, Vision-and-Language Navigation, and Place Navigation, evaluated through executable robot interfaces rather than teleport-style waypoint checks.
  • Zero-shot comparison across RL, VLA, and modular baselines shows no single paradigm dominates: end-to-end VLAs get the highest raw success, but the modular baseline gives the strongest safety profile.
  • The Place Navigation task shows a sharp performance drop moving from pure outdoor to indoor-to-outdoor scenes, pinpointing cross-context adaptation — not indoor or outdoor navigation individually — as the bottleneck current agents haven't solved.
Show abstract

Robots deployed in delivery, campus, and emergency-response settings often need to navigate from buildings to streets within a single continuous episode. Existing benchmarks usually evaluate indoor and outdoor navigation separately, and many abstract away robot execution, leaving exit finding, boundary traversal, adaptation, and kinodynamic failures underexplored. We introduce NavVerse, a physics-enabled benchmark for indoor-to-outdoor embodied navigation. NavVerse contains 100 indoor scenes, 50 urban outdoor scenes, and 50 indoor-to-outdoor scenes, and 10,000 episodes spanning Object Navigation, Vision-and-Language Navigation, and Place Navigation tasks, where agents search for semantic points of interest such as restaurants or banks. Agents are evaluated through executable robot interfaces using task-success, path-efficiency, and safety metrics. Zero-shot experiments with RL, VLA, and modular baselines show that current agents remain far from solving cross-context navigation: end-to-end VLAs obtain the highest zero-shot success, while the modular method provides the strongest safety profile. PlaceNav further reveals a clear drop from outdoor to indoor-to-outdoor scenes, indicating that adaptation remains major bottleneck.

EA-Nav: Learning Safe Visual Navigation Policies with Embodiment Awareness

2026-07-22 cs.RO, cs.CV Jituo Li (h=15)
Jialu Zhang, Yong Du, Xianda Guo, Shunwang Sun, Xinqi Liu
Core Contributions
  • Addresses a specific ambiguity in cross-embodiment navigation: identical visual input implies different valid actions for different robot bodies, which vision-only imitation can't resolve — the paper injects embodiment geometry as explicit conditioning tokens rather than hoping the network infers it implicitly.
  • Builds a cross-embodiment pretraining dataset from Internet videos, sidestepping reinforcement learning's need for large-scale interaction and hand-designed reward shaping used by prior cross-embodiment navigation work.
  • Fine-tuning uses a decoupled architecture with a trajectory-augmentation strategy that manufactures high-risk samples specifically to train spatial perception and risk-aware correction as separate signals, rather than hoping safety emerges from nominal trajectories alone.
  • Demonstrates consistent navigation improvements across different embodiment settings, evidence that explicit geometric conditioning — not just more imitation data — closes the cross-embodiment gap.
Show abstract

Cross-embodiment navigation is a key challenge in embodied intelligence. Due to differences in embodiment, the same visual observation may imply different actions for different agents, making prediction ambiguous when relying solely on vision. Existing studies mainly rely on reinforcement learning, which requires large-scale interaction and careful reward design, making it difficult to support scalable pretraining and real-world adaptation. In contrast, imitation-learning-based approaches remain limited. To address these challenges, we propose an imitation-learning-based embodiment-aware navigation framework with a modular multi-stage design. In pretraining, we construct a cross-embodiment navigation dataset from Internet videos and introduce embodiment geometry as conditional tokens to reduce action ambiguity under the same observation. In fine-tuning, we design a multimodal information injection mechanism based on a decoupled architecture. Specifically, we design a trajectory augmentation strategy to generate high-risk samples, which are used to train spatial perception and risk-aware correction separately, thereby explicitly incorporating embodiment geometry for safe navigation. Experimental results show that the proposed method effectively improves navigation performance across different embodiment settings, demonstrating the effectiveness of incorporating embodiment geometry into embodied navigation.

A two-stage mixed-integer linear programming framework is introduced for subsea pipeline incident response planning, jointly optimizing Subsea Docking Plate (SDP) placement and resident autonomous underwater vehicle allocation to minimize both maximum and average response times under spatial uncertainty. Phase 1 minimizes the maximum response time across all potential leak locations. Phase 2 reduces the average response time subject to the maximum bound. A case study based on the Johan Sverdrup oil and gas field in Norway, with 9 SDP options and 33 pipelines, shows that just a few well-placed SDPs are enough to achieve top performance. A cost versus time Pareto analysis reveals the trade-off between deployment expenditure and response efficiency, providing actionable guidance for designing resilient and cost-effective subsea inspection networks.

Ensuring safety in Physical AI systems operating in real-world environments is a critical challenge, particularly in hospital wards where vulnerable patients, clinical staff, medical devices, and assistive robots coexist. In this paper, we reinterpret Clinical Pathways as explicit runtime safety specifications for embodied medical AI. We propose a conceptual robotic architecture that integrates wearable sensors, smart medical devices, and assistive robotic components into a unified framework for real-time safety monitoring. At its core, a Runtime Safety Monitor (RSM) evaluates multimodal physiological and system-level signals against clinically defined constraints derived from the prescribed care process.
